Analytical procedures play a crucial role in obtaining substantive audit evidence, as outlined in the preceding chapter on auditing standards. 
These procedures are generally categorized into two methodological classes: regression and classification. 
The key distinction lies in the nature of the target variable, continuous for regression (e.g., monetary amounts) and categorical for classification (e.g., binary outcomes or class membership).

This chapter focuses on regression analysis, beginning with the fundamentals of simple linear regression. 
In this context, we examine the relationship between a single independent variable and a continuous dependent variable, assuming a linear functional form. 
Where linearity is untenable, appropriate transformations of variables are proposed to better capture the underlying structure.

Following the discussion on simple regression, we extend our analysis to multiple regression, incorporating several independent variables to model more complex relationships and improve explanatory power.

Regression-based analytical procedures are used to evaluate financial data by identifying unusual or unexpected items, those that deviate significantly from predicted values based on historical patterns or relationships. 
Such anomalies may warrant further investigation to determine their nature and cause. 
In the absence of notable deviations, auditors assess the overall fit of the model and the precision of its predictions to establish the level of assurance provided, thereby forming a basis for concluding on the reasonableness of the financial data.

\section{What is regression analysis}
\label{sec:what_is_regression_analysis}
\subsection{Introduction to regression analysis} % (fold)
\label{sub:quick_overview}

Regression analysis is a frequently applied statistical technique that serves as a basis for studying and characterizing the relationship between a \hlblue{response variable}\index{response variable}\index{variable!response} or \hlblue{dependent variable}\index{dependent variable}\index{variable!dependent} and one or more \hlblue{predictors}\index{predictor}\index{variable!predictor} or \hlblue{independent variables}\index{independent variable}\index{variable!independent}. 

% Exercise \ref{ex:summarizing_and_plotting}
Figure \ref{fig:H7_scatterplot} shows a scatter plot of the independent variable production ($x$-axis) and the dependent variable revenue ($y$-axis) for the \textit{Case: US SteamCo}. 
The plot is an easy means to get a feel for the relationship. 

The scatter plot reveals a clear positive (ascending) relationship between production and revenue, which aligns with expectations based on our understanding of the business relationship between steam production and revenue, further supporting the suitability of a linear regression model.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_scatterplot.pdf}
  \caption{Scatter plot of production and revenue for US SteamCo.}
  \label{fig:H7_scatterplot}
\end{figure}


We capture the relationship between the dependent variable $y$\nomenclature{$y$}{dependent variable} and independent variable $x$\nomenclature{$x$}{independent variable} as follows:

\begin{equation}
  y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon
  \label{eq:simple_linear_regression_model}
\end{equation}

Equation \ref{eq:simple_linear_regression_model} defines a \hlblue{simple linear regression model}\index{simple linear regression model}\index{regression model!simple linear}. 
Variable $y$ on the left-hand side of the equation is the conditional mean of the variable under analysis, or the dependent variable. Variable $x$ on the right side represents the independent variable. The relationship between $y$ and $x$ is \hlblue{linear}\index{linear relationship}, meaning that a change of one unit in $x$ has an effect of $\beta_1$ on $y$, regardless of the number of units $x$. We call  $\beta_1$ the \hlblue{slope coefficient}\index{slope coefficient}\index{slope} in the relationship between $y$ and $x$. The \hlblue{intercept coefficient}\index{intercept coefficient}\index{intercept}, sometimes referred to as the \hlblue{constant}\index{constant}, is required for mathematical purposes. The \hlblue{error}\index{error} or \hlblue{disturbance}\index{disturbance} term $\epsilon$ \nomenclature{$\epsilon$}{error or disturbance term} indicates that this is not an exact equality relationship.

Because the data in \textit{Case: US SteamCo} incorporate a time dimension, we refer to the regression analysis as a \hlblue{time-series analysis}\index{time-series analysis}. 
In this case, additional analyses must be performed. Otherwise, the analysis is referred to as a \hlblue{cross-sectional analysis}\index{cross-sectional analysis}. 
One of the applications is when an analysis is carried out over a large number of companies in the same industry or over a group of outlets of a retailer, for example, in the \textit{Case: On the Go Stores}.

% subsection quick_overview (end)

\subsection{Purposes of regression analysis} % (fold)
\label{sub:purposes_of_regression_analysis}

Auditors may use regression analysis for the following purposes:

\begin{itemize}
\item Investigate whether the two variables are correlated, and what the strength of the relationship is.

For example, we may want to determine whether there is a relationship between production and revenue in the \textit{Case: US SteamCo}.

\item Identify outliers during the estimation period.

For example, when comparing historical monthly revenue with the amount of steam produced, we may observe some months that are different from most other months. Further investigation may reveal information regarding the inaccuracy of the data or other independent variables that need to be added to the model. 

\item Create expectations for the hold-out period.

Once a relationship has been established and the predictability of this relationship has been assessed, we can use this relationship to predict monthly revenue from the known level of production.

\item Identify the variables that could explain the variation in the dependent variable.

For example, when analyzing the month-to-month differences in revenue, how much of the observed variation can be explained by production and/or other relevant information.

\item Identify observations in the hold-out period that require further investigation.

The expectations for monthly revenue are expected to be distributed over a certain range. The width of this range depends on the strength of the model. If the observed revenue is outside this range, the auditor should investigate the cause of the unexpected result.

\item Direct or reduce other substantive audit procedures.

After completing the analysis and follow-up work on unexpected differences, we will have a better understanding of this part of the financial statements and amend our audit approach accordingly.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
The initial phase of data analysis, referred to as an \hlblue{exploratory data analysis}\index{exploratory data analysis}, is aimed at understanding the dataset and identifying patterns, relationships, anomalies, and potential insights. Exploratory data analysis involves a variety of techniques and visualizations to summarize and examine the data. Some common techniques include the following:

\paragraph{Summary statistics:} Calculating measures like mean, median, standard deviation, etc., to understand the central tendency and spread of the data.
\paragraph{Data visualization:} Creating plots like histograms, scatter plots, box plots, and density plots to visualize the distribution and relationships among variables.
\paragraph{Data cleaning:} Identifying and handling missing values, outliers, and inconsistencies in the dataset.
\paragraph{Feature selection:} Identifying relevant variables for analysis and modeling.
\paragraph{Hypothesis generation:} Forming initial hypotheses about potential relationships or patterns that can guide further investigation.

\bigskip
\hlblue{Confirmatory data analysis}\index{confirmatory data analysis} is a more formal and structured approach to data analysis that comes after the exploratory phase. 
The primary goal of confirmatory data analysis is to test specific hypotheses or research questions by using predefined statistical methods. 
This phase is characterized by a higher level of hypothesis testing and validation. Some key aspects of the confirmatory phase include the following:

\paragraph{Hypothesis testing:} Applying statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA, chi-square tests) to assess the significance of relationships or differences among variables.
\paragraph{Model fitting:} Building and evaluating predictive models based on established hypotheses and theoretical frameworks.
\paragraph{Multiple comparisons correction:} Adjusting statistical tests to account for the increased risk of false positives when performing multiple comparisons.
\paragraph{Statistical inference:} Drawing conclusions and making generalizations about the population based on the sample data.
\paragraph{Prespecified analysis plan:} Following a predetermined plan to avoid data-driven bias and maintain the integrity of the analysis.

\bigskip
As can be seen from the above examples, regression analysis can be used as an exploratory and a confirmatory data analysis technique.

\subsection{Regression as Audit Evidence}
\label{sub:regression_as_audit_evidence}

In an audit context, regression analysis is not performed for the purpose of estimating coefficients as such, but as a means of obtaining audit evidence. The technique allows the auditor to formalise relationships observed in the data and to use these relationships to form expectations, identify unusual observations, and assess the plausibility of recorded amounts.

More specifically, regression analysis may be used to address questions such as whether observed relationships are consistent with the underlying business processes, which factors plausibly explain variation in the data, and what values would reasonably have been expected under normal circumstances. In this sense, a regression model serves as a structured representation of the auditor’s understanding of the entity and its environment.

The usefulness of such a model does not depend solely on the ability to estimate it, but on the extent to which it can be relied upon. Before the results of a regression analysis can be used as audit evidence, the auditor must evaluate whether the model provides a sufficiently reliable basis for drawing conclusions. This evaluation requires consideration of the quality and relevance of the data, the validity of the assumptions underlying the model, and the presence of alternative explanations for the observed patterns.

The procedures discussed in this chapter—ranging from model specification and estimation to residual analysis, influence diagnostics, and the construction of prediction intervals—should be viewed in this light. Each of these procedures contributes to the assessment of whether the model can be used to support an audit conclusion.

Regression analysis does not eliminate uncertainty. Rather, it provides a disciplined framework within which uncertainty can be analysed and communicated. The role of the auditor is therefore not to accept the output of a model uncritically, but to evaluate it in the context of the audit, taking into account both its strengths and its limitations.

Accordingly, the central question that underlies this chapter is not whether a regression model can be estimated, but whether it can be relied upon as a source of audit evidence in the exercise of professional judgement.

\section{Assumptions underlying regression results}
\label{sec:assumptions_underlying_regression_results}

% ### **7.2. Assumption Underlying Regression Results**
The calculation underlying the regression results presented in this chapter is based on a number of assumptions, some of which need to be satisfied to obtain \emph{unbiased estimates} of the slope and intercept coefficients, and others to allow conventional statistical tests of the model parameters and the creation of a prediction interval around the expectation. The resulting model and prediction uncertainty may then be used as evidence in the separate audit hypothesis test.

It is important to make this distinction: if the first group of assumptions is satisfied but one or more from the second group are violated, we still have a valid regression model that can serve the purposes of an explanatory data analysis. If the model is to produce prediction intervals and serve as audit evidence, all assumptions relevant to those predictions need to be validated. This model validation remains distinct from evaluating the recorded amount under the audit hypotheses.

We first set out to test the assumptions that need to be satisfied to obtain unbiased estimates. The testing assumptions for the regression inference are discussed in Section \ref{sec:testing_model_assumptions}.

\bigskip
The four assumptions that must be satisfied to obtain unbiased estimates of the coefficients of the regression model are

\begin{itemize}
\item linearity in the parameter (Section \ref{sub:linearity});
\item random sampling (Section \ref{sub:random_sampling});
\item sample variation in the independent variable (Section \ref{sub:sample_variation_in_the_independent_variable}); and
\item zero-conditional mean (Section \ref{sub:zero_conditional_mean}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Linearity} % (fold)
\label{sub:linearity}

The linearity assumption implies that the relationship between the dependent variable $y$ and the independent variable $x$ can be described by the regression model in Equation \ref{eq:simple_linear_regression_model}:

\begin{equation*}
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon
\end{equation*}
where $\beta_0$ is the intercept coefficient, $\beta_1$ is the slope coefficient, and $\epsilon$ represents the error or disturbance term.
\nomenclature{$\beta_0$}{intercept coefficient}
\nomenclature{$\beta_1$}{slope coefficient}

This linearity ensures that the change in the dependent variable is directly proportional to the change in the independent variable, with a constant rate of change represented by the coefficient. 

It may be difficult to find relationships that are truly linear. However, deviations from linearity may be small for certain intervals of the independent variable, or we can achieve linearity in the coefficients after transformation\footnote{For transformations, see Section \ref{sub:transformations}} of the values of the independent variable.
 
% subsection linearity (end)


\subsection{Random sampling} % (fold)
\label{sub:random_sampling}

A requirement that is often overlooked is that the observations of $y$ and $x$ used to estimate the coefficients $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are randomly obtained. Particularly in time-series analysis, it may be difficult to see the observations of $y$ and $x$ in consecutive periods as a random sample, and some cross-sectional samples cannot be deemed to be such.

If observations are not randomly obtained, this can lead to the following problems:

\paragraph{Systematic bias:} The coefficients might systematically over- or under-estimate the true relationships.
\paragraph{Spurious relationships:} In time-series data or non-random cross-sectional samples, patterns may emerge that are not reflective of true causation but rather of sampling bias or autocorrelation.
\paragraph{Invalid predictions:} Predictions based on a biased sample may fail to generalize to other populations or contexts.

\bigskip
To overcome these problems, we may conduct bootstrap or Monte Carlo simulations to assess the robustness of results to potential sampling biases, test the sensitivity of the results to changes in the sample or assumptions about randomness, or transparently report the limitations of the sampling method and the potential impact on results.

% subsection random_sampling (end)

\subsection{Sample variation in the independent variable} % (fold)
\label{sub:sample_variation_in_the_independent_variable}

To study the effect of a change in the independent variable on the dependent variable, we should observe different values of the independent variable. If all values of the independent variable are the same, then nothing can be concluded about the effect of changes in the independent variable on the dependent variable.

This issue is closely related to what we will discuss when identifying \emph{outliers in $x$}: with no variation in $x$, we have no way of telling anything about a possible relationship, and if the range of $x$ values is narrow, the basis for any opinion is also narrow. Moreover, the narrow base of $x$-values may be a good linear approximation over that range; however, extending that range when creating expectations may violate the linearity assumption.

% subsection sample_variation_in_the_independent_variable (end)

\subsection{Zero-conditional mean} % (fold)
\label{sub:zero_conditional_mean}

This requirement is quite technical, and we usually do not have to worry about it. First, it is needed to ensure that the estimators of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are unbiased. The requirement that the errors $\epsilon$ have a mean of zero is met if we include the constant $\beta_0$ in the regression model. The expanded requirement is that the mean of the errors should not depend on the values of the independent variable. 

The effect of this requirement is that

\begin{equation}
E(y | x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1x
\end{equation}

This means that the value of $y$ given a certain value of $x$ can be expressed as a linear function, and that the increase in $x$ by one unit increases the expected value of $y$ by the amount of $\beta_1$, regardless of the value of $x$.

In most applications of regression analysis in auditing, the zero-conditional mean assumption is not something to worry about excessively if the model is well specified and the key variables are included. However, in high-stakes analyses or when making causal claims, ensuring that this assumption holds is critical to producing valid results. Tools such as residual analysis and robustness checks can help verify its validity. These tools are beyond the scope of this book.

% subsection zero_conditional_mean (end)

Every data analysis endeavor hinges on the acquisition, description, and preprocessing of data, constituting a foundational phase that profoundly influences the subsequent analytical outcomes. This process is integral in ensuring the quality, accuracy, and reliability of the data used in the analysis. In our experience, this step of data analysis is often the most time-consuming.

Acquisition involves sourcing data from diverse and pertinent sources such as databases, surveys, sensors, and web scraping. The choice of data sources must be guided by the research objectives and the context of the analysis. Careful consideration is required to ensure that the collected data align with the research questions and possess the attributes necessary to address the objectives.

We list the dataset(s) acquired together with their locations, the methods used to acquire them, and any problems encountered. We record the problems encountered and any resolution achieved. This will aid in the future replication of this project and execution of similar future projects.

Describing data involves cataloging various attributes, dimensions, and characteristics. This step is crucial for gaining an understanding of the structure, range, and potential limitations of data. Metadata, which provides contextual information about the data, including its origin, format, and units of measurement, is essential for comprehending the data's context and reliability. Accurate documentation aids in preventing erroneous interpretations and enhances the analysis transparency. The documentation includes the format of the data, quantity of data (e.g., number of records and fields in each table), identities of the fields, and any other surface features that we discovered. We evaluate whether the acquired data satisfies the relevant requirements.

Preprocessing plays an instrumental role in preparing the data for analysis. It encompasses various tasks including data cleaning, transformation, and normalization. Data cleaning involves identifying and rectifying inaccuracies, missing values, outliers, and inconsistencies that could skew the results. Transformation involves converting data into a suitable format for analysis, which may entail aggregating, encoding, or standardizing the variables. Normalization ensures that variables with differing scales or ranges are placed on a common scale, thereby preventing undue influence from variables with larger magnitudes.

The significance of this process is highlighted by its critical influence on the validity of analytical outcomes. Poorly acquired data, inadequate descriptions, or negligent preprocessing can lead to flawed insights and erroneous conclusions. Therefore, we must exercise due diligence and follow established best practices to ensure data integrity.

The histogram is a graphical tool used to summarize the distribution of a dataset. 
It is constructed by dividing the range of values into a series of intervals, or bins, which are typically of equal width. 
For each bin, the number of data points that fall within its range is counted, and this frequency is then represented by the height of a vertical bar. 
The bins are arranged along the horizontal axis, while the frequencies (or relative frequencies) are plotted on the vertical axis. 
This simple yet effective layout makes it easy to see how data are distributed across the range of values.

Histograms serve several important purposes in data analysis. They offer a clear visual summary of the shape and spread of a dataset, making it easier to detect skewness, symmetry, or deviations from a normal distribution. Patterns such as clusters, gaps, and peaks become readily apparent, and potential \hlblue{outliers}\index{outlier} (values that fall far outside the main distribution) can often be identified at a glance. 
Histograms are also useful for comparing datasets, either by overlaying multiple plots or by combining them with other visual tools such as box plots. 
More generally, they support decision-making and data preprocessing by highlighting important features of the data that may suggest the need for transformations, adjustments in bin width, or other analytical steps.

\subsection{Testing independent data}
\label{sub:testing_independent_data}

Examining data for accuracy and completeness holds a crucial position in the data analysis process, guaranteeing the reliability and credibility of the insights and conclusions drawn from the data. Various approaches can be employed to ascertain data quality.

Data profiling involves scrutinizing data to determine its structure, patterns, and distributions. This includes the identification of missing values, outliers, and anomalies. Profiling serves to highlight potential data-quality concerns, necessitating further inquiry.

Data cleaning involves rectifying, eliminating, or filling missing or erroneous data points. This process involves actions such as imputing missing values, rectifying typographical errors, and addressing inconsistencies in formatting.

Data validation verifies whether the data adhere to pre-established rules, constraints, or business logic. For instance, validating the appropriateness of date ranges or verifying numerical values against specific limits.

Cross-checking involves comparing data from different sources or credible references to identify inconsistencies. When the same information is present in multiple datasets, discrepancies may signal potential data-quality issues.

Data completeness entails ensuring the presence of all essential data fields for each record. The absence of data can skew the analytical outcomes and lead to inaccurate deductions.

Duplicate detection aims to pinpoint and eliminate duplicate records that can artificially elevate the significance of specific values and disrupt the analysis.

Data consistency ensures that identical data values are consistently represented throughout the dataset. Inconsistencies in labeling or formatting can contribute to confusion and errors in the analysis.

Domain knowledge validation involves engaging experts who are well versed in the relevant field. They validate data accuracy and recognize potential issues that might evade detection through data analysis alone.

\bigskip
Working with untested or poor-quality data can have several negative effects on data analyses.

\paragraph{Inaccurate insights} Unreliable data can lead to inaccurate conclusions and insights. Decision-making based on inaccurate data can result in poor strategies and outcomes.
\paragraph{Biased results} Data quality issues such as missing values or skewed distributions can introduce bias into the analysis, leading to skewed results and misleading interpretations.
\paragraph{Wasted resources}
Analyzing poor-quality data is an inefficient use of time and resources. Moreover, inaccurate conclusions may lead to misguided audit efforts.
\paragraph{Damaged reputation} If decisions are made based on faulty data, they can damage the reputation of individuals or organizations, especially if the decisions lead to negative outcomes.
\paragraph{Misguided strategies} Data-driven strategies based on unreliable data can lead to incorrect business directions, missed opportunities, or increased risks.

\subsection{Data wrangling} % (fold)
\label{sub:data_wrangling}

Data wrangling involves various operations to prepare and refine data for analysis. \hlblue{Constructing data}\index{constructing data}\index{data!constructing} involves creating new attributes (derived attributes) or generating entirely new records with transformed values from the existing attributes. For instance, derived attributes are formed by combining existing attributes within the same record, such as calculating the area using the formula "length times width."

For the \textit{Case: US SteamCo},\footnote{AAG-DATA B.31} we create a \hlblue{dummy variable}\index{dummy variable} to distinguish between heating months (October to April) and cooling months (May to September). 
Such a variable would have the value 1 in heating months and 0 in cooling months. 
The effect of this variable on the trajectory of the regression line is explained in Section \ref{sub:disaggregation_ch07}.

\hlblue{Integrating}\index{data!integration} data, which is another aspect of data wrangling, focuses on combining information from multiple sources. 
This can include merging tables that contain different aspects of the same object. For example, a retail chain's store information, sales data, and demographic data can be merged to form a new comprehensive table with the fields from each source. 
\hlblue{Data aggregation}\index{data aggregation}\index{aggregation!data} is also a part of data integration, which involves the computation of new values by summarizing data across records or tables. 
For instance, in the \emph{Case: US SteamCo} it is imaginable that the variables \ttblue{coolDD} and \ttblue{heatDD} were constructed by first calculating the daily differences between maximum temperature and reference temperature, and then adding these differences for the month.

\hlblue{Formatting data}\index{formatting data}\index{data!formatting} is another crucial step, involving syntactic mod\-i\-fications that maintain data meaning but meet the requirements of the modeling tools. 
This can include reordering attributes to match tool specifications because some tools demand a specific order or random arrangement of records. 
Additionally, formatting might include syntactic changes to fulfill specific tool demands, such as removing commas from text fields or limiting the length of values to adhere to tool constraints.

Neglecting data wrangling can lead to inaccuracies and misinterpretation in the analysis. We ensure the accuracy, relevance, and suitability of the data for modeling by effectively constructing, integrating, and formatting data, ultimately improving the reliability of the analytical outcomes.

\subsection{Estimation set and hold-out set} % (fold)
\label{sub:estimation_set_and_hold_out_set}

We typically distinguish between the observations used to establish the relationship (the \hlblue{estimation set}\index{estimation set}) and those used to create expectations (the \hlblue{hold-out set}\index{hold-out set}). 
For time-series data, this split is straightforward: we use prior year(s) data for the estimation set to develop the model. The model is then used to make expectations for the current year, where the current-year data form the hold-out set. For the \emph{Case: US SteamCo}, we use the data from 2011 to 2013 as the estimation set and the data for 2014 as the hold-out set.

When dealing with cross-sectional data, the distinction between the estimation set and the hold-out set (used to create expectations) is less obvious than in time-series data because the observations do not follow a natural temporal order. However, we can still establish a framework to compare predictions with actual outcomes by adopting strategies depending on the type of data and context.

When we analyze cross-sectional data from similar entities, such as retail outlets or stores, the data can be split into an estimation set and a hold-out set based on random sampling or structural characteristics. In the random approach, we randomly split the data into the estimation set and the hold-out set. For instance, if we have data on 90 retail outlets, we use 60 outlets to estimate the model and reserve 30 outlets for testing. The hold-out period, in this case, is represented by the data on the remaining outlets, and we can directly compare the predicted values (based on the model estimated on the first set) with the actual outcomes in the hold-out set. This process is repeated three times to ensure that each outlet is used once as part of the hold-out set, allowing us to evaluate the performance of the model across all outlets and to ensure the robustness of the predictions.

If the outlets can be grouped based on structural characteristics (e.g., by location, size, or sales volume), we can use one group as the estimation period and another group as the hold-out period. For example, we define the estimation set as the data on outlets in smaller towns to estimate the model, and the hold-out set as the data on outlets from large cities to validate the model.
By splitting the data this way, you ensure that the model is evaluated on entities that have not been used for estimation, but are still comparable because they belong to the same population. A drawback of this approach is that the two groups could exhibit different patterns, resulting in expectations that are significantly different from the recorded values.

\bigskip
In situations where the entities are not comparable or not enough similar entities are available (e.g., unique real estate properties in a housing corporation), we need to adopt alternative approaches.

For example, we can use cross-validation to assess the model’s accuracy, such as \hlblue{K-fold cross-validation}\index{K-fold cross-validation}. 
In this technique, the dataset is split into $K$ equal parts.\nomenclature{$K$}{number of parts in K-fold cross-validation}
The model is trained on $K-1$ parts and validated on the remaining part. 
This process is repeated $K$ times with different parts as the hold-out set each time. 
This method allows you to evaluate the model's performance without needing comparable entities.
For small datasets, we can use \hlblue{leave-one-out cross-validation}\index{leave-one-out cross-validation}, where the model is trained on all data except one observation, and the prediction is tested on that single observation. 
This is repeated for each observation, giving a robust measure of model performance without needing a separate hold-out set.

\subsection{Model selection} % (fold)
\label{sub:model_selection}

There are many different modeling techniques, ranging from simple linear regression, with one dependent variable and one independent variable, to structural equation modeling, which accommodates multiple dependent variables and multiple independent variables. We may have already formed an idea about which technique to use when deciding which data to collect, but there may be reasons to choose an alternative when the selected modeling technique does not produce a satisfactory result. For example:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the resulting model is imprecise;

  When the model is imprecise, the desired level of assurance may not be obtained from the analysis. This could be caused by regression outliers (see Section \ref{sub:outliers_and_influential_observations}), a level of aggregation of the data that is too high, or an omitted independent variable;

  \item one or more of the model assumptions failed; or

  For example, when the normality test fails, we may decide to apply a transformation to the dependent variable or remove regression outliers. When there is evidence of non-constant variance and/or autocorrelation, we may apply WLS, GLS, or GARCH\footnote{See Section \ref{sub:alternative_modeling_techniques}} techniques.

  \item it appears that a nonlinear equation fits the data better.

  Particularly in multiple linear regression, nonlinearity in one of the independent variables could only be detected by executing a transformation.\footnote{For transformations, see Section \ref{sub:transformations}}
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
Our general recommendation is to begin with a simple model. 
While simple linear regression may not fully capture the complexity of the underlying data-generating process, it can often provide a sufficiently accurate approximation for the purpose at hand, such as testing whether the dependent variable is materially misstated. 
Starting with a basic specification also facilitates interpretation and helps build intuition. 
In the course of this chapter, we progressively extend the initial model to better accommodate the structure and characteristics of the data. 
In \ttblue{mod.1}, we introduce multiple independent variables to account for additional explanatory factors. \ttblue{mod.2} builds on this by incorporating interaction terms, allowing for differential effects of predictors across conditions (e.g., between summer and non-summer periods). 
In \ttblue{mod.3}, we refit the model after addressing an influential outlier, thereby improving robustness. Subsequently, \ttblue{mod.4} adjusts for autocorrelation identified in the residuals, which, if left uncorrected, could compromise the validity of inference. 
Finally, in \ttblue{mod.5}, we return to a \hlblue{winsorized} version of the dataset and apply a stepwise model selection procedure to remove irrelevant predictors, thereby enhancing model parsimony without sacrificing explanatory power.

\subsection{Simple linear regression} % (fold)
\label{sub:simple_linear_regression}

First, we focus on the linear relationship between revenue and production. Figure \ref{fig:H7_Enhanced_scatterplot} shows that there is a positive (increasing) relationship: as production increases, so does revenue. 

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_enhanced_scatterplot2.pdf}
  \caption{Enhanced scatterplot of the data in the estimation set of US SteamCo.}
  \label{fig:H7_Enhanced_scatterplot}
\end{figure}

To capture this relationship in a straight line, we need to estimate the two coefficients of the line. We introduced the regression model in Equation \ref{eq:simple_linear_regression_model} as
\begin{equation*}
  y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon
  % \label{eq:simple_linear_regression_model}
\end{equation*}
\noindent where $\beta_0$ is the \hlblue{intercept}\index{intercept}, the place where the regression line crosses the $y$-axis at $x = 0$, and $\beta_1$ is the \hlblue{slope}\index{slope} of the regression line. 
The coefficients are estimated such that the line best fits the data in the \emph{estimation period}\index{estimation period}. 
$\hat{y}$ is the estimated value of the conditional mean of the dependent variable $y$, conditional on the realized value of $x$.
Because real-world data rarely fall perfectly on a straight line, the regression model includes an error term $\epsilon$, which represents the random variation or noise in $y$ that cannot be explained by the linear relationship with $x$. 
This term captures all other factors affecting $y$ that are not explicitly included in the model.

Note that the observations are scattered around the (presumed) straight line. Apparently, the relationship between revenue and production is not clear. Numerous factors can lead to this inaccuracy. One factor is the absence of other independent variables in the model; this is something that we expand on in Section \ref{sub:multiple_regression}. The relationship may not be linear but can be better expressed by a curve; this is investigated in Section \ref{sub:transformations}. There could be structural changes to this relationship. The time-series plot in Figure \ref{fig:H7_Time_series_plots} also indicates seasonality in the data. It remains to be seen at this point whether or not all seasonality in the dependent variable can be explained by seasonality in the independent variable, or should be taken care of by other means. 

All the uncertainties referred to above are captured in the single \hlblue{residual}\index{residual} $\epsilon$ in the regression model. 
We want to minimize this residual when estimating the coefficients from the sampled observations. For this purpose we introduce the \hlblue{regression line}\index{regression line}

\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x
\label{eq:regression_line}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\begin{tabbing}
\hspace*{1.2cm}\=\hspace*{0.4cm}\=\hspace*{0.2cm}\= \kill
where \> $\hat{y}$ \> : \> estimate of the conditional mean $y$ of the dependent variable \\
      \> $x$     \> : \> value of the independent variable \\
      \> $b_0$     \> : \> estimated intercept \\
      \> $b_1$     \> : \> estimated slope
\end{tabbing}

The coefficients are estimated such that the line best fits the data in the \hlblue{estimation period}\index{estimation period}. 

The method of estimating the coefficients is referred to as \hlblue{Ordinary Least Squares}\index{ordinary least squares} or \hlblue{OLS}\index{OLS}. 
In this method, the regression line is positioned between the points in the scatter plot such that the quadratic vertical distance between the line ($\hat{y}$) and each of the observations ($y_i$), aggregated over all observations, is minimized. 
As a result of this minimization procedure, the regression line always passes through the point ($\bar{x}, \bar{y}$), where $\bar{x}$\nomenclature{$\bar{x}$}{mean value of the independent variable $x$} and $\bar{y}$\nomenclature{$\bar{y}$}{mean value of the dependent variable $y$} are the means of the independent and dependent variables, respectively. 
This property ensures that the fitted line represents the average relationship between the variables in the dataset.

To establish the equation of the line, both the slope and the constant term need to be calculated. 
These parameters must be determined in a manner that aligns the line as closely as possible with the sample data from 2011 to 2013 (the estimation period). It is imperative to recognize that the line is derived from sample data, and it can be aptly referred to as a \hlblue{sample line}\index{sample line}. 
If the line were to be calculated using data from different years, its trajectory would likely differ. 
Hence, the slope coefficient $b_1$ is a sample statistic and estimate of the slope coefficient $\beta_1$ of the (unknown) population. 
The population can be conceptualized as encompassing all measurable monthly figures. 
Similarly, the constant term $b_0$ in the equation represents a sample statistic that is an estimate of the population constant $\beta_0$. 

\bigskip
The values of $b_0$ and $b_1$ obtained with the method of \emph{OLS} are

\begin{equation}
b_1 = \frac{\sum{xy} - n \bar{x} \bar{y}}{(n - 1) s^2_x}
\label{eq:slope_coefficient}
\end{equation}

\noindent and

\begin{equation}
b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}
\label{eq:intercept_coefficient}
\end{equation}

\noindent
% \begin{tabbing}
% \hspace*{1.2cm}\=\hspace*{0.4cm}\=\hspace*{0.2cm}\= \kill
% where \> $\bar{x}$ \> : \> mean value of the independent variable $x$ \\
%       \> $\bar{y}$ \> : \> mean value of the dependent variable $y$ \\
%       \> $s_x$     \> : \> standard deviation of $x$
% \end{tabbing}

\begin{tabular}{ll}
$\bar{x}$ & mean value of the independent variable $x$ \\
$\bar{y}$ & mean value of the dependent variable $y$ \\
$s_x$     & standard deviation of $x$
\end{tabular}

For the \emph{Case: US SteamCo}, we use \ttblue{production} in 1,000 lb for the $x$-values, and \ttblue{revenue} in \$ for $y$ to construct our first model \ttblue{mod.0}.
The estimated values are $b_0 = 4,897,663$ for the intercept and $b_1 = 18.99$ for the slope.\footnote{The calculation of the coefficients is shown in Exercise \ref{ex:modelling_mod0}.} Replacing the coefficients in Equation \ref{eq:regression_line} with their estimates we obtain the regression line

\begin{equation}

\label{eq:regression_model_0}
\end{equation}

This shows that the company earns \$18.99 in revenue for every additional thousand pounds of steam produced. We also observe that the \hlblue{coefficient of determination}\index{coefficient of determination} $r^2$\nomenclature{$r^2$}{coefficient of determination} is equal to 0.397.\footnote{See Exercise \ref{ex:summary.mod.0}} We already encountered this statistic in Equation \ref{eq:coefficient_of_determination}.

\subsection{Multiple regression} % (fold)
\label{sub:multiple_regression}

We extend the simple linear regression model introduced in Equation \ref{eq:simple_linear_regression_model} by adding additional independent variables. The extended model can be written as

\begin{equation}
 y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x_1+ \ldots + \beta_kx_k+ \epsilon
 \label{eq:multiple_linear_regression_model} 
\end{equation}

The main reason for extending the model is to improve the model fit, as measured by $r^2$. A better model fit, in turn, will lead to more precise expectations and, ultimately, a higher level of assurance obtained from the analysis. One of the problems related to omitted significant variables is that they could lead to biased and inconsistent estimates of the coefficients included in the model.

In practice, it is not always easy to determine which variables should be considered for inclusion in a model or to obtain their data. When we are fortunate enough to have data for all potentially relevant independent variables, we may not always be able to use all of them. As a general guideline, data scientists recommend using at least 10 to 20 observations for every independent variable included in the model. Some stricter guidelines suggest a minimum of 30 observations per variable to improve stability and reduce overfitting. This implies that with an estimation set containing three years of monthly data ($n = 36$), we can deploy between one and three independent variables, or at most three under more lenient rules. 

When using too few observations per variable, the model may suffer from \hlblue{overfitting}\index{overfitting}, where it captures noise instead of the underlying relationship, resulting in poor generalization to new data. Additionally, the coefficients of the regression model may become unstable and biased, as the model struggles to estimate the effects of variables accurately. These problems emphasize the importance of balancing model complexity with the size of the dataset to ensure reliable and interpretable results. The question then arises: when we have more independent variables available, how can we choose a subset with a maximum of two or three variables that best fits the data?

There are different strategies to select independent variables for inclusion in the model. 
These methods differ in the way they add or remove independent variables from the model and the statistical criteria they use to make these decisions. 
A graphical strategy is to review the Correlogram in Figure \ref{fig:H7_Correlogram}.

\bigskip
The \hlblue{enter method}\index{enter method} is the most straightforward approach, in which all candidate independent variables are entered simultaneously into the model. The \emph{enter} method does not involve any variable selection or elimination, and all independent variables are included in the final model. This method is often used when all candidate independent variables are considered important or necessary for predicting the outcome variable. In contrast, \emph{backward} and \emph{forward} methods are stepwise approaches that involve selecting a subset of independent variables from a larger pool of candidate independent variables.

The \hlblue{backward method}\index{backward method} starts with a model that contains all potential independent variables and then systematically removes independent variables individually that do not contribute significantly to the model's prediction power. This process continues until all remaining variables are statistically significant, based on a predetermined criterion. Common criteria include $p$ value thresholds, $F$ statistics, or information-based metrics such as the \hlblue{Akaike Information Criterion}\index{Akaike information criterion}\index{AIC} (AIC) or \hlblue{Bayesian Information Criterion}\index{Bayesian information criterion}\index{BIC} (BIC). These criteria are under the control of the data scientist, who selects thresholds or metrics appropriate for the analysis. For example, a $p$ value threshold of 0.05 might be chosen to ensure that only variables with strong evidence of association remain in the model. These criteria are discussed in detail in Sections \ref{sec:analysis_of_variance} and \ref{sec:testing_significance}.

The \hlblue{forward method}\index{forward method} starts with an empty model and adds independent variables one by one based on a statistical criterion such as the highest correlation coefficient, the largest $F$ statistic, or the lowest BIC. The process stops when no additional variable significantly improves the model’s prediction power, according to the chosen criterion. Similar to the backward method, the choice of criterion is controlled by the data scientist and tailored to the specific context of the analysis.

The combination of these two methods, often referred to as \hlblue{stepwise selection}, alternates between adding and removing variables to refine the model. In stepwise selection, the data scientist must define both inclusion and exclusion thresholds based on statistical criteria. For example, a variable might be added to the model if its $p$ value is below 0.05 and removed if its $p$ value rises above 0.10 after subsequent adjustments.

These metrics suggest that the inclusion of relevant variables enhances the explanatory power of the model, demonstrating the importance of carefully chosen criteria in stepwise selection methods. \subsection{Transformations} % (fold)
\label{sub:transformations}

Transforming variables in linear regression serves several key purposes, each aimed at enhancing the model's performance, accuracy, and interpretation of results. As discussed in Sections \ref{sec:assumptions_underlying_regression_results} and \ref{sec:testing_model_assumptions}, linear regression relies on several critical assumptions, including linearity of the relationship between variables, \hlblue{homoskedasticity}\index{homoskedasticity} (constant variance), and normality of residuals. These assumptions are foundational for obtaining unbiased estimates of the model parameters, valid statistical tests, and reliable predictions. Violations of these assumptions, such as non-linearity, heteroskedasticity, or skewness, can compromise the validity of the regression model and its inferences.

Transformations offer a practical solution to address these issues. They can adjust the variables to meet the assumptions required for linear regression, ensuring the model is suitable for robust analysis. In Section \ref{sec:testing_model_assumptions}, these assumptions are systematically tested, and the role of transformations in satisfying them is evaluated. Below, we outline the primary purposes of transforming variables in linear regression. Each purpose aligns with specific model assumptions and aims to improve model reliability and interpretability.

\bigskip
Both the dependent variable $y$ and independent variables $x_1 \ldots x_k$ can be transformed. The transformation of the dependent variable $y$ is referred to as the \hlblue{Box-Cox transformation}\index{Box-Cox transformation}\index{transformation!Box-Cox}, a mathematical technique using parameter lambda ($\lambda$)\nomenclature{$\lambda$}{parameter of the Box-Cox or Box-Tidwell transformation}, which can take different values to achieve various transformations. The general formula for the Box-Cox transformation is:

\begin{equation}
y(\lambda) =    
\begin{cases}
  \frac{y^\lambda - 1}{\lambda} & \text{if } \lambda \neq 0 \\    
  \ln(y) & \text{if } \lambda = 0 \\   
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

In this formula, $y$ represents the original data point and $\lambda$ is the transformation parameter. $y(\lambda)$ represents the transformed value of the data point using the Box-Cox transformation with the parameter $\lambda$. As $\lambda$ approaches zero, the formula approaches the natural logarithm. As $\lambda$ approaches one, the formula approaches a simple linear transformation $y(\lambda) = y - 1$ (i.e., the identity transformation).

\bigskip
The interpretation of some values of $\lambda$ is as follows.

  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = -1:} This corresponds to the reciprocal transformation, $y(\lambda) = 1 - 1/y$. It can be used when the data have a decreasing exponential pattern.
  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = -0.5:} This corresponds to the negative square root transformation, $y(\lambda) = 2(1-\frac{1}{\sqrt{y}})$. This is useful for data that exhibit a square-root relationship.
  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = 0:} This corresponds to the natural logarithm transformation, $ y(\lambda) = \ln(y)$. It is commonly used to stabilize the variance of data and make it more normally distributed.
  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = 0.5:} This corresponds to the square root transformation, $ y(\lambda) = 2(\sqrt{y} - 1)$. It is suitable for data with a square root relationship.
  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = 1:} This corresponds to the identity transformation, $ y(\lambda) = y - 1$. No transformation is applied in this case.
  \paragraph{$\lambda$ = 2:} This corresponds to the square transformation, $ y(\lambda) = (y^2 - 1)/2 $. It is used when the data have a quadratic relationship.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_exponential_function_pos.pdf}
  \caption{Exponential function $y = e^{b_0} \cdot e^{b_1x}$ and its transformation $ln(y) = b_0 + b_1x$. The slope coefficient $b_1$ is positive.}
  \label{fig:H7_exponential_function_pos}
\end{figure}

Table \ref{tab:list_of_common_transformation_methods} on page \pageref{tab:list_of_common_transformation_methods} summarizes commonly used transformations.

\bigskip
Choosing an appropriate $\lambda$ involves finding the transformation that best stabilizes the variance, normalizes the distribution, and achieves linearity for the specific dataset. This process typically requires maximizing the likelihood function of the transformed data, which can be accomplished using computer software such as \ttblue{R}. For instance, \ttblue{R} provides functions like \ttblue{boxcox} from the \ttblue{MASS} package, which automates the estimation of the optimal $\lambda$ value based on the given data and regression model. These tools simplify the search for the $\lambda$ that optimizes the model's fit, ensuring the process is efficient and user-friendly.

The Box-Cox transformation assumes that the data values are positive. If the data contain zeros or negative values, alternative transformations like the \hlblue{Yeo-Johnson transformation}\index{Yeo-Johnson transformation} can be used, which can accommodate a wider range of data distributions.

\bigskip
The \hlblue{Box--Tidwell transformation}\index{Box-Tidwell transformation}\index{transformation!Box-Tidwell} models non-linear relationships by applying a power transformation to the predictor variables \(x_i\), rather than to the response variable. It is defined as:
\begin{equation}
x_i(\lambda_i) = x_i^{\lambda_i}
\end{equation} 
where $\lambda_i$ is a parameter estimated for each predictor $x_i$, allowing for individual transformations of the predictors. The Box-Tidwell transformation is often applied when one or more predictor variables exhibit non-linear relationships with the response. The main advantage transforming the predictor variables rather than the dependent are interpretability of the model, particularly with regard to concluding on whether the account analyzed is materially misstated.

The choice of transformation depends on the characteristics of the data and goals of the analysis. However, it is important to note that although transformations can offer benefits, they can also introduce complexity and complicate the interpretation of results. It is essential to carefully consider the trade-offs and underlying assumptions before applying transformations in linear regression modeling.

\subsection{Alternative modeling techniques} % (fold)
\label{sub:alternative_modeling_techniques}

Simple and multiple linear regression models and non-linear models are easy to understand and interpret and cover the majority of applications in financial statement audits. Occasionally, we need to resort to more sophisticated techniques that have been developed to address either failing model assumption tests or more specific data structures, particularly for time-series data. We now provide a brief overview of alternative techniques. They will not be discussed in detail; the interested reader is referred to the references in Chapter \ref{chap:foundations_and_further_reading}.

\paragraph{Weighted Least Squares} % (fold)
\label{par:weighted_least_squares}

\index{weighted least squares}  (WLS)\index{WLS} is a modeling technique in which each observation is assigned a weight that is proportional to the inverse of its variance. The weights adjust the contribution of each observation to the regression line and assign more weight to observations with smaller variances. WLS assumes that the errors follow a normal distribution with constant variance, and the method is most effective when the variance of the errors is related to a known variable.

% paragraph weighted_least_squares (end)

\paragraph{Generalized Least Squares} % (fold)
\label{par:generalized_least_squares}

\index{generalized least squares} (GLS)\index{GLS} is a technique that uses weighted least squares to estimate the coefficients of the regression model, with weights based on an estimated covariance matrix that considers both heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation.

% paragraph generalized_least_squares (end)

\paragraph{GARCH\index{GARCH} or Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity} % (fold)
\label{par:generalized_autoregressive_conditional_heteroskedasticity}

\index{generalized autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity} models are based on time series models and then generalized to accommodate non-constant variance. 

% paragraph generalized_autoregressive_conditional_heteroskedasticity (end)

\paragraph{Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average} % (fold)
\label{par:autoregressive_integrated_moving_average}

\index{autoregressive integrated moving average} (ARIMA)\index{ARIMA} models are uni\-variate time-series models that use the past values of a single variable to predict future values. They assume that the future values of a time series are a function of its past values and error terms. ARIMA models consist of three components: autoregressive (AR), integrated (I), and moving average (MA) components. The AR component captures the relationship between past and future values, the I component deals with nonstationary data, and the MA component deals with the error term. ARIMA models are best suited to stationary data and are commonly used for short-term forecasting.

Extensions of ARIMA are \hlblue{Seasonal ARIMA}\index{seasonal ARIMA} (SARIMA)\index{SARIMA} models, that incorporate seasonal effects, and \hlblue{eXogenous ARIMA}\index{exogenous ARIMA} (X-ARIMA)\index{X-ARIMA} models, that incorporate values of one or more independent variables.

% paragraph autoregressive_integrated_moving_average (end)

\paragraph{Vector Autoregressive} % (fold)
\label{par:vector_autoregression}

\index{vector autoregressive} (VAR)\index{VAR} models are multivariate time series models that use the past values of multiple variables to make predictions about future values. VAR models assume that each variable in the system is a function of its past values and the past values of the other variables in the system. VAR models do not require data to be stationary and are useful for modeling the dynamic interrelationships between multiple variables over time.

Unlike the X-ARIMA models, VAR models do not explicitly incorporate exogenous variables. Instead, they assume that each variable in the system is influenced by past values of all other variables in the system. VAR models are best suited to time-series data with multiple variables that may influence each other over time.

% paragraph vector_autoregression (end)

\paragraph{Robust regression} % (fold)
\label{par:robust_regression}

\index{robust regression} techniques, such as GLS, address issues such as heteroskedasticity and regression outliers in the data; however, it is a non-parametric method that does not assume any specific form for the distribution of the errors or the covariance structure. It is based on a robust estimator of the regression coefficients, such as the M-estimator or the S-estimator, which downweights the influence of outliers in the data. Robust regression is a more flexible method than GLS and can be useful when the data contain influential observations (see Section \ref{sub:outliers_and_influential_observations}).

% paragraph robust_regression (end)



\paragraph{Bayesian Structural Time Series} % (fold)
\label{par:bayesian_structural_time_series}

\index{Bayesian structural time series}  (BSTS)\index{BSTS} is a Bayesian approach to time-series analysis that can be used for forecasting, decomposition of time series into components, and causal inference.

BSTS models can adjust for heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, and non-normality of the regression residuals by modeling the errors as a combination of both autoregressive process and stochastic volatility processes. The autoregressive process models autocorrelation in the residuals, whereas the stochastic volatility process models the time-varying variance of the residuals.

% paragraph bayesian_structural_time_series (end)

\subsection{Outliers and influential observations}
\label{sub:outliers_and_influential_observations}

After estimating the regression relationship, we first examine whether there are unusual observations. They can be problematic because they may exert considerable influence on the estimated coefficients in the regression model and also because they may indicate that some features of the data are not represented in the model. Moreover, the least-squares method is very sensitive to violations of  underlying model assumptions.

\bigskip
\noindent We distinguish between:

\begin{itemize}
\item \hlblue{leverage points}\index{leverage point}, which are individual data points that have extreme values on a combination of independent variables;
\item \hlblue{regression outliers}\index{regression outlier}, individual data points that have an observed value that is far different from the fitted value; and
\item \hlblue{influential observations}\index{influential observation}, observations that are outliers and have high leverage.
\end{itemize}

Figure \ref{fig:H7-influenceIndexPlot} summarizes the influence statistics. It shows from bottom to top the hat values indicating potential leverage, Studentized residuals indicating regression outliers, and their respective Bonferroni $p$ values and Cook's distance, indicating potentially influential observations. Each of these is discussed in detail.

\begin{figure}[bhtp]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_influenceIndexPlot.pdf}
  \caption{Influence index plots for \ttblue{mod.2}. From bottom to top: Leverage points, regression outliers, their respective Bonferroni $p$ values and influential observations.}
  \label{fig:H7-influenceIndexPlot}
\end{figure}


\paragraph{Leverage points} Leverage points or outliers in $x$ have an extreme value on a combination of independent variables. The effect of these points is that they may have an outsized influence on the position or slope of the regression line. Leverage is measured by the \hlblue{hat values}\index{hat value}\nomenclature{$h_i$}{hat value for observation $i$}, that indicate how far a data point is from the average of all the other points in the dataset. Specifically, the hat values represent the diagonal elements of the \hlblue{hat matrix}\index{hat matrix}, which is used to compute the predicted values of the dependent variable $y$ based on the values of the independent variable(s) $x$. 

In case of one single independent variable,\footnote{The formula for hat-values for models with multiple predictors is more convoluted, but can be elegantly written with matrix algebra. This is beyond the scope of this book.} for example in \newline \noindent \ttblue{mod.0}, the hat-values are calculated as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hat_value}
  h_i = \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_j - \bar{x})^2}
\end{equation}
\nomenclature{$j$}{index}

Equation \ref{eq:hat_value} shows that $h_i$ increases as the distance between $x_i$ and $\bar{x}$ increases. Therefore, in simple linear regression high-leverage observations lie outside the normal range of observations. An extension of Equation \ref{eq:hat_value} to cases with $k$\nomenclature{$k$}{number of independent variables} independent variables requires matrix algebra, and we do not provide the formula here. The leverage statistic $h_i$ is always greater than $1 / n$ (if $x_i = \bar{x}$) and less than 1, and the sum of all hat values $\sum h_i = k + 1 $. As a result, hat values that are much greater than the average, $(k + 1) / n$, may require further investigation.

\paragraph{Regression outliers} A regression outlier, or outlier in $y$, is an observation in which the value of the dependent variable is conditionally unusual, given the value of the independent variable. Given that the distance to the regression line is much shorter for the other variables, it should cause us to wonder why this observation is different. The most serious problem related to regression outliers is that they violate the assumption that residuals are normally distributed.

Ignoring outliers may cause the model's precision to be overestimated, resulting in insufficient audit evidence. Conversely, if an outlier is removed from the analysis (or assigned a lower weight, as in robust regression\footnote{See page \pageref{par:robust_regression}.}) and similar circumstances occur during the period under audit, the model may underestimate the true variability, resulting in unnecessary additional audit work.

Regression outliers are identified using the \hlblue{Studentized residual}\index{Studentized residual}. 
Studentized residuals are preferred over standardized residuals for detecting regression outliers because they account for the leverage and influence of each data point, adjusting for variations in residual variance. 
The Studentized residual is calculated as

\begin{equation}
		e_i^* = \frac{e_i}{s_{e(-i)}\sqrt{1 - h_i}}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}llX@{}}

% \begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright}p{1.2 cm}p{1cm}p{9.3 cm}}
where: & $e_i^*$ : & the Studentized residual of observation $i$\\
& $e_i$ : & the residual of observation $i$\\
& $s_{e(-i)}$ : & the standard deviation of all observations except $i$\\
& $h_i$ : & the leverage of observation $i$\\
\end{tabularx}
\nomenclature{$e_i$}{the residual of observation $i$}
\nomenclature{$e_i^*$}{the Studentized residual of observation $i$}

\bigskip
The Studentized residual $e_i^*$ follows a $t$ distribution with $n - k - 2$ degrees of freedom. Be careful, however, to look up all observations with a Studentized residual greater than 2. First, we expect to find approximately 5\% of the observations above this boundary. Secondly, when we want to identify the largest Studentized residual, we employ the $t$-test $n$ times, thereby heavily capitalizing the test probabilities.\footnote{For example, if we carry out a hypothesis test 20 times with an $\alpha$ risk of 5\%, the effective $\alpha$ risk will be $1 - 0.95^{20} = 0.64.$} A solution to this is to consider the number of tests. This procedure is referred to as the \hlblue{Bonferroni adjustment}\index{Bonferroni adjustment}. It takes the $p$ value for the largest absolute Studentized residual and multiplies it with the number of tests $n$, in the \emph{Case: US SteamCo} $n = 36$. 

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_qqplot.pdf}
  \caption{QQ plot for \ttblue{mod.2}, with the three largest Studentized residuals identified.}
  \label{fig:H7_qqplot}
\end{figure}

Our first response to regression outliers is to examine the accuracy of the input data and further investigation of the circumstances surrounding the observation. When the analysis is cross-sectional and the outlier is the result of an unusual situation that is not expected in the current period under audit, we may remove it. This is not a good solution when dealing with time-series data because removing a data point will then cause problems with regard to the time-series properties of the model, such as autocorrelation. In such cases, we may use \emph{robust regression}\index{robust regression}\index{regression!robust}. 

\bigskip
For \emph{Case: US SteamCo}, Figure \ref{fig:H7_qqplot} shows the \hlblue{QQ plot}\index{QQ plot} of the residuals. 
The $x$-axis represents the values of a $t$-distribution corresponding to the sorted data points, meaning it shows how the data compares to the expected distribution of the $t$-statistics.
The $y$-axis represents the Studentized residuals. The blue line represents perfect fit. 

We can observe in Figure \ref{fig:H7_qqplot} that the unadjusted $p$ value of the largest absolute Studentized residual is multiplied by the number of tests $n = 36$ to arrive at the Bonferroni $p$ value. Since the Bonferroni $p$ value is small, we conclude that observation 22 is unusual and should be further examined.

\paragraph{Influential observations} An observation that has a disproportionate influence on regression coefficients is referred to as an influential observation. These observations have a combination of high leverage and high Studentized residuals.

We accommodate influential observations by either applying robust regression, or leaving these observations out when estimating the regression coefficients. \hlblue{Cook's distance}\index{Cook's distance} $D$ is an influence statistic that combines leverage and the level of outlyingness. It measures the shift in the coefficient estimate by fitting a regression model when that particular observation is omitted. The statistic is defined for the $i$th observation as

\begin{equation}
D_i = \frac{e_i^{*2}}{k+1}\frac{h_i}{1-h_i}
\end{equation}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}llX@{}}
where: &$D_i$ & : Cook's distance for observation $i$\\
&$e_i^*$ & : the Studentized residual of observation $i$\\
& $k$ & : the number of independent variables in the model\\
& $h_i$ & : the leverage of observation $i$\\
\end{tabularx}
\nomenclature{$D_i$}{Cook's distance for observation $i$}

\bigskip
Cook's distance follows an $F$ distribution with $k$ and $n - k$ degrees of freedom. However, some caution should be exercised in finding an appropriate cut-off value to identify influential observations: if the cut-off is chosen too low (for example, $D < \frac{4}{n - k -1}$), points that exercise only mild influence are highlighted for further scrutiny, thereby underestimating the precision of the model. This results in a higher occurrence of observations in the current audit period that have an unacceptable difference, leading to additional work for the auditor. If the cut-off is chosen too high (for example $D > 1$), the model coefficients are distorted, with ultimately the same effect. 
As a general rule, a Cook's distance greater than 1 indicates a point likely to have substantial influence on the model, while a point in the 0.5 to 1.0 range is only occasionally worthy of investigation. Observations with Cook's distance below 0.5 are unlikely to influence the model in any substantial way.

There are no specific explanations for the unusually low values in June 2013 and October 2013. However, the observation from October 2012 coincides with a known disruption in production due to Hurricane Sandy, which struck the East Coast of the United States.\footnote{See also Figure \ref{fig:H7_scat_rev2} on page \pageref{fig:H7_scat_rev2}} This extreme event resulted in a Studentizized residual of approximately $-4.168$, marking it as a clear outlier in the dataset.

Since we are working with time-series data, we cannot remove the observation without disrupting the temporal structure of the analysis. Instead, we apply residual winsorization to reduce the influence of this extreme value while retaining its presence in the series. In this approach, the residual is adjusted relative to the fitted regression value. Specifically, the residual for October 2012 is replaced by the 5th percentile of the residual distribution, and the adjusted response is reconstructed as the fitted value plus this winsorized residual.

Mathematically, \hlblue{residual winsorization}\index{winsorization!residual} first decomposes the observed response into a fitted value and a residual,
\begin{equation}
e_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t.
\end{equation}
For lower and upper residual winsorization bounds $\tau_L$ and $\tau_U$ \nomenclature{$\tau_L$, $\tau_U$}{lower and upper residual winsorization bound}, the winsorized residual $e_t^\ast$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
e_t^\ast =
\begin{cases}
\tau_L & \text{if } e_t < \tau_L, \\
e_t & \text{if } \tau_L \le e_t \le \tau_U, \\
\tau_U & \text{if } e_t > \tau_U.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:residual_winsorization}
\end{equation}
The adjusted response is then reconstructed as
\begin{equation}
y_t^\ast = \hat{y}_t + e_t^\ast.
\label{eq:adjusted_response}
\end{equation}
In our case, only the October 2012 residual was below the lower threshold $\tau_L$, defined as the 5th percentile of the residual distribution. The adjusted October 2012 revenue is therefore set equal to its fitted value plus the 5th-percentile residual. This ensures that the outlier does not exert undue influence on the regression estimates while preserving the time structure of the data.

\subsection{Multicollinearity} % (fold)
\label{sub:multicollinearity}

Multicollinearity is a phenomenon in linear regression models in which independent variables are highly correlated with each other, leading to unstable and unreliable results. This can be particularly problematic when interaction terms are present, as they tend to increase the correlation between variables.

Multicollinearity should be checked using \hlblue{Variance Inflation Factor}\index{variance inflation factor} (VIF) calculations for each predictor. This factor quantifies how much the variance of the estimated coefficient is inflated due to multicollinearity with other predictors. For a predictor $x_j$, VIF is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
  VIF(x_j) = \frac{1}{1 - r_j^2}
\end{equation}
where $r_j^2$ is the $r^2$ value obtained by regressing $x_j$ on all the other independent variables in the model. 

High VIF scores, particularly above 10, indicate the presence of multicollinearity and the need for action. If VIF > 10 for a particular predictor $x_j$, this implies that $r_j^2 > 0.9$. 
If multicollinearity is detected, one approach is to remove one of the independent variables from the model, thereby reducing the correlation between the remaining independent variables.

Alternatively, we can consider transforming the independent variables themselves, such as by taking the logarithm of one or both variables, to reduce multicollinearity.
Another effective strategy involves employing regularization techniques such as ridge or lasso regression. These techniques can reduce the regression coefficients to zero, thereby mitigating the impact of multicollinearity.

In some cases, if multicollinearity persists as a significant problem, we may explore alternative models that are less sensitive to multicollinearity, such as decision trees or random forests. These models are more robust and reliable when dealing with highly correlated variables.

Ignoring multicollinearity can lead to serious issues in linear regression models. First, the coefficients become unreliable and challenging to interpret, as they can change drastically with minor adjustments to the model or data. Incorrect standard errors may also be estimated, affecting the computation of confidence intervals and hypothesis tests, leading to misleading conclusions regarding the significance of independent variables. Moreover, multicollinearity can increase the risk of overfitting the model, causing it to perform poorly on data from the hold-out set.

\bigskip
In regression models that include interaction terms, the \hlblue{Generalized Variance Inflation Factor}\index{generalized variance inflation factor} (GVIF) provides a more appropriate measure of multicollinearity than the standard VIF. The GVIF is especially useful when a predictor is represented by more than one degree of freedom, for example because it is categorical or because it is involved in interaction terms.

The raw GVIF is not directly comparable across predictors with different degrees of freedom. For that reason, software often reports the adjusted quantity

\[
GVIF_j^{1/(2df_j)}.
\]

This adjustment places predictors with different degrees of freedom on a comparable scale. However, it should be interpreted carefully. For a one-degree-of-freedom predictor, the adjusted GVIF equals the square root of the ordinary VIF:

\[
GVIF_j^{1/(2df_j)} = \sqrt{VIF_j}.
\]

Consequently, the common rule of thumb that a VIF greater than 10 indicates serious multicollinearity corresponds to an adjusted GVIF greater than approximately

\[
\sqrt{10} \approx 3.16.
\]

Thus, when using the adjusted GVIF scale, values above about 3.16 may indicate substantial multicollinearity. Values well below this threshold do not suggest serious variance inflation.
For the \emph{Case: US SteamCo}, the largest VIF in \ttblue{mod.1} is 5.60 (for \ttblue{coolDD}), and the largest adjusted GVIF in \ttblue{mod.3} is 2.47 (also for \ttblue{coolDD}). Both are below their respective reference thresholds, so we find no evidence of problematic multicollinearity in the working models.

\section{Analysis of variance} % (fold)
\label{sec:analysis_of_variance}

% ### **7.6. Analysis of Variance**
In this section, we focus on the model quality. If one of the purposes of regression analysis is to explain the variation in the dependent variable, \hlblue{analysis of variance}\index{analysis of variance} or \emph{anova}\index{anova} assists in describing how each independent variable explains this variation and how much remains unexplained. 

In statistical analysis, the terms \emph{variation} and \emph{variance} are frequently encountered, each carrying distinct implications despite their apparent similarity. While both concepts pertain to the dispersion or spread of data points around a central tendency, they diverge subtly in their mathematical formulations and interpretations. Specifically, \emph{total variation} denotes the aggregate of squared deviations between individual data points ($y_i$) and the sample mean ($\bar{y}$), encapsulating the entirety of variability within a dataset. In contrast, \emph{variance} represents a refined measure, derived from the total variation, wherein the summation of squared deviations is divided by the degrees of freedom, $n - 1$. This adjustment compensates for the tendency of sample variance to underestimate the population variance, thereby yielding an unbiased estimator. Consequently, while both metrics elucidate the extent of dispersion in a dataset, the incorporation of degrees of freedom renders variance a more nuanced and statistically robust measure compared to total variation.

Note that $s^2_y = TSS/(n-1)$, and that therefore $TSS = s^2_y (n - 1)$.

We can now try to make statements about the quality of the relationship. One way of doing this is by means of the correlation between $x$ and $y$, expressed in the \hlblue{correlation coefficient}\index{correlation coefficient}, that we introduced in Equation \ref{eq:correlation}

\begin{equation*}
r = b_1 \frac{s_x}{s_y}
\end{equation*}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}llX@{}}
where: &$b_1$ : & the slope coefficient of the regression line\\
& $s_x$ : & the estimated standard deviation of the independent variable $x$\\
& $s_y$ : & the estimated standard deviation of the dependent variable $y$\\
\end{tabularx}

\bigskip
The correlation coefficient $r$ takes values between -1 and +1. The sign of the coefficient indicates the direction of the relationship and the numerical value indicates the strength. The correlation is perfect when the coefficient equals -1 or +1. Values close to these values suggest a strong relationship, whereas those values close to zero do not support an assumed linear relationship. 

When we square the correlation coefficient, the result is the \hlblue{coefficient of determination}, $r^2$. 
In the context of regression analysis, this quantity has a special interpretation: it represents the percentage of the variance explained by the model. 
The coefficient of determination can therefore also be expressed as the ratio between explained and total variation of the dependent variable $y$

\begin{equation}
  r^2 = \frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{TSS}} 
  % = \frac{\sum{(\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2}}{\sum{(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}
\end{equation}

In the \textit{Case: US SteamCo}, the total variation in the dependent variable, measured by the total squared distance between the observations $y_i$ and their average $\bar{y}$ is 

\begin{equation*}
\sum{(y_i - \bar{y})^2} = 9.5307 \cdot 10^{14}
\end{equation*}

\bigskip
The full summary table\footnote{See \ref{ex:summary.mod.0}} displays the values of the multiple $r^2$ (also referred to as the coefficient of determination\index{coefficient of determination}) and the adjusted $r^2$. Since $r^2$ explains the percentage variance explained by the model, it may be tempting to compare models using this statistic. It should be noted that when adding independent variables to the model, the value of $r^2$ cannot decrease even when non-significant independent variables are added.

To make a fair comparison, the \hlblue{adjusted $r^2$}\index{adjusted $r^2$} was developed. It considers the number of independent variables and penalizes the addition of independent variables that do not improve the overall performance of the model. 

\begin{equation}
  \text{adjusted } r^2 = r^2 - k \frac{1 - r^2}{n - k - 1}
\end{equation}
The adjusted $r^2$ for \ttblue{mod.0} with $n = 36$ and $k = 1$ is then
\begin{equation*}
  \text{adjusted } r^2 = 0.3967 - \frac{1 - 0.3967}{34} = 0.3789
\end{equation*}
For example, when we compare \ttblue{mod\_forward} from Exercise \ref{ex:modelling strategies} with \ttblue{mod.1} from Exercise \ref{ex:modelling_mod.1} we observe that the latter has a higher $r^2$ (0.8683) than the former (0.8667). The only difference between the two models is that \ttblue{mod.1} includes the variable \ttblue{coolDD}, whereas \ttblue{mod\_forward} does not. The penalty of including this non-significant variable is visible in the comparison of the adjusted $r^2$: 0.8559 for \ttblue{mod.1} versus 0.8586 for \ttblue{mod\_forward}.

\bigskip
Some researchers prefer to use the \hlblue{Akaike Information Criterion}\index{Akaike Information Criterion} (AIC)\index{AIC} or \hlblue{Bayesian Information Criterion}\index{Bayesian Information Criterion} (BIC)\index{BIC} to evaluate the performance of regression models. They are calculated as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:aic}
  AIC = n \cdot \text{ln}(SSE / n) + 2k 
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bic}
  BIC = n \cdot \text{ln}(SSE / n) + k \cdot \text{ln}(n)
\end{equation}

A model with a lower AIC or BIC value is preferred. 
Similar to the adjusted $r^2$, both the AIC and BIC penalize models for the inclusion of additional independent variables that do not significantly improve performance. 
The penalty for adding more independent variables is linear with $k$ for the AIC, and proportional to $k \cdot \ln(n)$ for the BIC, where $k$ is the number of predictors and $n$ is the sample size. 
Thus, while a lower Sum of Squared Errors (SSE) reduces the AIC and BIC, adding too many predictors can increase them due to the penalty term. 
These criteria therefore balance goodness-of-fit with \hlblue{parsimony}\index{parsimony}.

AIC and BIC values should only be compared for models fitted to the same response data. 
This point is important in the present chapter. Models \ttblue{mod.0}, \ttblue{model\_forward}, \ttblue{mod.1}, and \ttblue{mod.2} are fitted to the original estimation data. 
Model \ttblue{mod.3}, by contrast, is fitted after winsorizing observation 22. 
Because the response variable has been modified, the likelihoods underlying AIC and BIC are not directly comparable with those of the earlier models. The winsorized model should therefore be interpreted as a sensitivity analysis rather than as a competing model in the same AIC/BIC comparison.

In this context, Table \ref{tab:comparison_mod_0_2} shows that each extension beyond \ttblue{mod.0} substantially improves explanatory power.

This illustrates the trade-off between explanatory richness and parsimony. From an audit perspective, \ttblue{mod.2} remains important because the interaction terms capture the distinct revenue relationships in summer and non-summer months, which are consistent with the business model of US SteamCo.

After this comparison, we separately examine the effect of the outlying observation identified in the diagnostic analysis. Winsorizing observation 22 leads to \ttblue{mod.3}. The improvement in fit after winsorization is in\-for\-mative, but it should not be interpreted as an AIC/BIC improvement over \ttblue{mod.2}, because the model is fitted to a modified response variable. Instead, it supports the conclusion that the outlier had a material influence on the regression results and that further work on the treatment of this observation is warranted.

Following the AAG guidance (AAG A.63), we also consider standard errors and $t$ statistics from the individual model summaries (for example, Table \ref{tab:summary_mod_3}). 
In \ttblue{mod.3}, for instance, most predictors have statistically significant $t$ statistics whose absolute values exceed 2.0, consistent with the guidance that such values indicate a valid relationship between the predictor and the dependent variable. 
This supports confidence in the reliability of the regression coefficients. 
However, some variables (e.g., \ttblue{heatDD} and \ttblue{summer:heatDD}) show low $t$ statistics, suggesting they may not contribute meaningfully and could be candidates for removal in future model refinements (AAG B.03–B.04). 
Although \ttblue{summer} also has a low $t$ value, caution is advised before removing it, as it interacts with both \ttblue{production} and \ttblue{coolDD}, potentially influencing their effects.

The standard error of the regression, although not tabulated, should also be interpreted in light of materiality. For example, AAG A.64 notes that if the standard error is less than about 75\% of materiality, the model may be considered reliable for audit purposes. In our case, if the standard error of the best-fitting models falls within this range, it would further support reliance on the regression. Moreover, as we discuss in Section \ref{sec:auditor_response}, we use statistical theory to translate the standard error to the level of assurance that is obtained form the model, by specifically incorporating materiality.

In line with AAG A.66, we also emphasize judgment: statistical criteria can guide but not replace informed model evaluation. For example, the marginally higher BIC of \ttblue{mod.2} versus \ttblue{model\_forward} might be acceptable if the added variables improve predictive accuracy or enhance interpretability.

Finally, we note the potential for model validation through holdout samples or cross-validation, as discussed in the AAG (A.65). Re-estimating the regression on subsets of the data can help assess the model’s robustness and generalizability, particularly when models are used for substantive or audit-related conclusions.

\section{Testing model assumptions}
\label{sec:testing_model_assumptions}

% ### **7.7. Testing Model Assumptions**
We discuss the assumptions for obtaining unbiased estimates in Section \ref{sec:assumptions_underlying_regression_results}. The second set of assumptions supports conventional statistical inference about the model and the construction of prediction intervals. The three assumptions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item normality;
\item homoskedasticity; and
\item (for time-series analyses only) the absence of autocorrelation.
\end{itemize}

We can rely on graphical information for each of these assumptions, or perform objective statistical tests. The hypotheses in the following diagnostic tests concern properties of the regression residuals. They are conventional model-validation hypotheses, not the audit hypotheses concerning material misstatement of the recorded amount.


% section conditions_underlying_regression_results (end)

\subsection{Normality} % (fold)
\label{sub:normality}

% SvA 9.6.5
Now that we have resolved any potential problems related to the need for disaggregation, time lags, outliers, and influential observations, we can test the normality assumption. It states that the disturbance terms $\epsilon$ follow a normal distribution. This assumption is necessary to construct prediction intervals, and is also an assumption of the tests for homoskedasticity and autocorrelation.

Figure \ref{fig:H7_hist_normalized_residuals} shows a histogram of the standardized residuals of \ttblue{mod.3}, with an overlay of a standard normal distribution curve. At first glance it is difficult to accept that the residuals are normally distributed. However, the visual representation is dependent on the choice of the number and width of bins used, especially when the number of observations is relatively low. Our main concern regarding the use of visual information is that the information presented depends on the specific settings of the graph. Instead, we should ask ourselves the following question: if we sample 35 observations from a normal distribution, is it probable that the residuals are distributed in this way. Statistical tests are used to quantify these probabilities.

Another graphical tool for assessing the normality of residuals is the QQ plot, previously introduced in the context of regression diagnostics. Figure \ref{fig:H7_qqplot}, on page \pageref{fig:H7_qqplot}, presents an example based on the data from \ttblue{mod.2}. Compared to earlier versions, the plot for \ttblue{mod.3} now reflects the winsorization of observation 22, which no longer appears as an outlier.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_hist_standardized_residuals_mod3.pdf}
  \caption{Histogram of standardized residuals of the \ttblue{mod.3} model.}
  \label{fig:H7_hist_normalized_residuals}
\end{figure}

Many statistical tests address normality. The \hlblue{Shapiro--Wilk test}\index{Shapiro--Wilk test} has been shown to outperform any competitor. We follow the nine-step approach from Section \ref{sec:the_nine_step_approach}.

\paragraph{Research question} Is there a reason to believe that the residuals $\epsilon$ are not normally distributed?


\paragraph{Hypotheses} We specify the null hypothesis as


$H_0 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ are normally distributed.
%\label{}

\bigskip
\noindent That makes the alternative hypothesis

$H_1 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ are not normally distributed.


\paragraph{Assumptions} There are no assumptions underlying the test.

\paragraph{Test statistic} We use the \hlblue{$W$ statistic}\index{W statistic}\nomenclature{$W$}{Shapiro--Wilk test statistic}. 
Its calculation is quite complicated and based on order statistics. The $W$ statistic takes values between 0 and 1, with 1 being a perfect match with the normal distribution. 

\paragraph{Distribution} The $W$ statistic's distribution cannot be described by means of a familiar distribution.

\paragraph{Significance level} In regression analysis, we deviate from the standard approach of committing to a significance level ahead of time and then using a critical region. Instead, it is more informative to answer the following question: given the observed value of the $W$ statistic, what is the smallest significance level at which the null hypothesis would be rejected? This level is referred to as the \hlblue{$p$ value}\index{p value}\nomenclature{$p$}{significance level} for the test. The significance level for the $W$ statistic is calculated using approximations.

\paragraph{Value of test statistic} We use the Shapiro--Wilk test to obtain the value of the $W$ statistic and its associated $p$ value. For \ttblue{mod.3}, the value of the $W$ statistic is 0.9480, with a $p$ value of 0.09045.\footnote{See Exercise \ref{ex:normality_test}.}

\paragraph{Conclusion}

The $p$ value of the Shapiro--Wilk test is 0.09045. Con\-se\-quently, we do not reject the null hypothesis of normality at the conventional significance levels of 5\% or 1\%. The test therefore provides no evidence that the residuals deviate from a normal distribution.

\bigskip
In our experience, many situations involving non-normality of the residuals are caused by data-input errors. Hence, we recommend performing diagnostic checking and/or reviewing the necessity of transformations prior to testing the model assumptions. If non-normality is not remedied by these procedures, Weighted Least Squares (see Section \ref{sub:homoskedasticity}) may help. Other remedies include nonparametric regression models such as kernel regression and spline regression, or generalized linear models. These remedies are beyond the scope of this book.

% subsection normality (end)

\subsection{Homoskedasticity} % (fold)
\label{sub:homoskedasticity}

The \hlblue{homoskedasticity} assumption\index{homoskedasticity} states that the disturbance terms $\epsilon$ not only follow a normal distribution, but also that the variance of this normal distribution is constant and does not depend on the values of any independent variable $x_j$. Ignoring this assumption could have serious consequences when constructing prediction intervals. For example, when the variance of the residuals increases linearly with the value of one of the independent variables, the expectations related to smaller values of $x_j$ are more precise than those for larger values of $x_j$. Therefore, if we inadvertently assume that the variance is constant, the prediction intervals related to smaller values of $x_j$ are overstated, and those for larger values of $x_j$ are understated. 

We use the \ttblue{residualPlots} function to detect heteroskedasticity. Figure \ref{fig:H7_residualPlots3} shows an example for the \ttblue{mod.3} model. It is a variation of Figure \ref{fig:H7_residualPlots_mod2}, leaving out the plot against fitted values and the line that indicates a possible quadratic fit.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_residualPlots3.pdf}
  \caption{Residual plots for the \ttblue{mod.3} model.}
  \label{fig:H7_residualPlots3}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Research question} Is there a reason to believe that the residuals $\epsilon$ do not have constant variance, regardless of the values of the independent variables?


\paragraph{Hypotheses} We specify the null hypothesis as


$H_0 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ have constant variance.
% %\label{}

\bigskip
\noindent The alternative hypothesis is

$H_1 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ do not have constant variance.


\paragraph{Assumptions} The test assumes that the residuals $\epsilon$ are normally distributed.

\paragraph{Test statistic} The test statistic is obtained by fitting a new regression model on the squared residuals from the model

\begin{equation}
  e_i^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1x_{1, i} + \alpha_2x_{2, i} + \ldots + \alpha_kx_{k, i} + u_i
\end{equation}
where $e_i^2$ is the squared residual of observation $i$, and $x_{1, i}, x_{2, i}, \ldots x_{k, i}$ are the values of the $k$ independent variables for observation $i$. 

If any of the $k$ independent variables is responsible for non-constant variance, its related coefficient $\alpha$ will be significant, and $r^2$ will increase.
The Breusch-Pagan test statistic $BP$ \nomenclature{$BP$}{Breusch-Pagan test statistic} is then given by

\begin{equation}
  BP = nr^2
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the number of observations and $r^2$ is the coefficient of determination from the regression of $e_i^2$ on the independent variables.

\paragraph{Distribution} Under the null hypothesis of constant variance or homo\-skedasticity, the test statistic $BP$ follows a chi-squared distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Significance level} Again, we typically conclude based on the $p$ value of the test.

\paragraph{Value of test statistic} We obtain the value of the $BP$ statistic, 7.1466, and its related $p$ value, 0.0674.\footnote{See Exercise \ref{ex:breusch_pagan}}

\paragraph{Conclusion}

The $p$ value of the test statistic is 0.0674. We therefore fail to reject the null hypothesis at conventional significance levels below 0.0674, such as 5\% or 1\%. The model-validation test provides no evidence of non-constant variance at these levels.

\bigskip
In cases where the null hypothesis is rejected, and the residuals show non-constant variance, we use a technique referred to as \hlblue{Weighted Least Squares}\index{weighted least squares} (WLS)\index{WLS}. It involves specifying a weight matrix for the residuals based on our understanding of the data and the source of heteroskedasticity. The weight matrix is based on the variance of the residuals, with larger weights assigned to observations with smaller residuals.

\subsection{Absence of autocorrelation} % (fold)
\label{sub:absence_of_autocorrelation}

Finally, in time-series applications of linear regression, it is assumed that the disturbance term $\epsilon_t$ in period $t$ is not correlated with the disturbance terms from any preceding periods. Violations of this assumption, known as autocorrelation, can arise, for example, due to seasonal effects. In monthly data, this might manifest as $\epsilon_t$ being correlated with $\epsilon_{t-12}$, reflecting a yearly seasonal cycle.

To detect such autocorrelation, we use a \ttblue{pacf} plot. Figure \ref{fig:H7_pacf} shows an example for the model \ttblue{mod.3}.
The abbreviation \hlblue{PACF}\index{PACF} stands for the \textbf{P}artial \textbf{A}uto\textbf{C}orrelation \textbf{F}unction.

The PACF plot helps us identify the order of autocorrelation, where the term \emph{order} refers to the number of time lags between observations. 
For instance, autocorrelation of order 1 (AR(1)) means that each residual is correlated with the residual from one period earlier. 
Order 2 (AR(2)) refers to correlation with the residual two periods back, and so on.

What distinguishes the PACF from the standard autocorrelation function (ACF) is that it measures the correlation at each lag after removing the influence of all shorter lags. 
In essence, it isolates the direct contribution of each lag, allowing us to identify the specific order(s) at which autocorrelation is present.

In the PACF plot, each vertical bar corresponds to the partial autocorrelation at a specific lag. 
The horizontal dashed lines indicate approximate 95\% confidence bounds. 
If a bar extends beyond these bounds, the partial autocorrelation at that lag is considered statistically significant.

\bigskip
For example, in Figure \ref{fig:H7_pacf}, we observe that only the bar at lag 1 exceeds the critical boundary. 
This suggests that the residuals exhibit first-order autocorrelation (AR(1)), with no strong evidence of correlation at higher lags. 
If multiple bars exceeded the bounds, say, at lags 1 and 2, it could point to an AR(2) process. If all bars fall within the bounds, we conclude that the residuals are not significantly autocorrelated.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{H7_pacf.pdf}
  \caption{Partial autocorrelation plot for \ttblue{mod.3}. A significant spike is observed at lag 1.}
  \label{fig:H7_pacf}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Research question} Are the residuals $\epsilon$ autocorrelated?


\paragraph{Hypotheses} We specify the null hypothesis as


$H_0 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ are not autocorrelated.
% %\label{}

\bigskip
\noindent The alternative hypothesis is

$H_1 : $ The residuals $\epsilon$ are autocorrelated.


\paragraph{Assumptions} The test assumes that the residuals $\epsilon$ are normally distributed.

\paragraph{Test statistic} The test statistic is obtained by fitting an autoregressive model of order $p$.\footnote{The order $p$ of the autoregressive model should not be confused with the $p$ generally used as probability of success.} The Breusch-Godfrey test statistic $BG$ \nomenclature{$BG$}{Breusch-Godfrey test statistic} is then given by

\begin{equation}
  BG = nr^2
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the number of observations, and $r^2$ is the coefficient of determination from the autoregressive model.

\paragraph{Distribution} Under the null hypothesis of no autocorrelation, the Breusch--Godfrey test statistic is asymptotically chi-squared distributed with $p$ degrees of freedom, where $p$ is the number of lagged residual terms included in the auxiliary regression.

\paragraph{Significance level} We conclude based on the $p$ value of the test. The null hypothesis is rejected when the chosen significance level is greater than the reported $p$ value.

\paragraph{Value of test statistic} We obtain a Breusch--Godfrey test statistic of 7.1466, with a related $p$ value of 0.06737.\footnote{See Exercise \ref{ex:breusch_godfrey_test}.} In this example, the test is performed with order $p=3$, so the reference distribution is a chi-squared distribution with 3 degrees of freedom. It is noteworthy that the order $p$ can be specified by the user. If omitted, the default value is \ttblue{order = 1}.

\paragraph{Conclusion}

The $p$ value of the test statistic is 0.06737. Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis at conventional significance levels below 0.06737, such as 5\% or 1\%. The model-validation test provides no evidence of autocorrelation at these levels, although the result is marginal at a 10\% level.
This is visually supported by a moderate spike in the PACF plot. 
While not definitively conclusive, these signs suggest that the model may benefit from adjustments to account for autocorrelation. 
In time-series regression, residual autocorrelation violates the assumption that the error terms are independent over time. When this occurs, the ordinary least squares coefficient estimates may still be unbiased if the regressors are exogenous, but the usual standard errors, confidence intervals, and significance tests may no longer be reliable.

One commonly suggested workaround is to include lagged residuals as predictors in the model. 
This approach can be effective for addressing first-order autocorrelation, but it comes with a caveat: since residuals are themselves estimates derived from the data, treating them as fixed predictors introduces a layer of uncertainty that is typically not accounted for. As a result, the method lacks a rigorous statistical foundation and may lead to biased or inefficient estimates if used uncritically.

A more principled solution is to apply \hlblue{Generalized Least Squares}\index{generalized least squares} (GLS), which adjusts the estimation procedure by explicitly modeling the covariance structure of the residuals. However, GLS is beyond the scope of this book.

\bigskip
Instead, a practical and widely used alternative is to apply a first-order autoregressive correction. This approach estimates the autocorrelation parameter and transforms the regression model by subtracting a multiple of the lagged value from both the dependent variable and each independent variable. The transformed model can then be estimated by ordinary least squares, restoring valid statistical inference when the residuals follow an AR(1) process.

For a regression model without interaction terms, the transformed model is given by

\begin{equation}
  y_t - \rho y_{t-1}
  =
  \beta_0(1-\rho)
  + \beta_1(x_{1,t}-\rho x_{1,t-1})
  + \cdots
  + \beta_k(x_{k,t}-\rho x_{k,t-1})
  + u_t,
\end{equation}
where $\rho$\nomenclature{$\rho$}{estimated autocorrelation coefficient} is the estimated autocorrelation coefficient, and $u_t$\nomenclature{$u_t$}{transformed error term} is the trans\-formed error term.

\bigskip
When the regression model includes interaction terms, the interaction variables should first be constructed and then transformed in the same manner as the other predictors. For example, if

\[
z_t = x_t s_t
\]
is an interaction term, the transformed interaction becomes

\begin{equation}
  z_t - \rho z_{t-1}
  =
  x_t s_t - \rho x_{t-1}s_{t-1}.
\end{equation}

Thus, the interaction term itself is transformed, rather than multiplying separately transformed predictor variables.

\bigskip
This transformation reduces the first-order autocorrelation in the residuals, provided that the AR(1) model is appropriate. The transformed variables are then used to estimate the regression model by ordinary least squares. In this chapter, the resulting model is \ttblue{mod.4}, for which the coefficient estimates, their standard errors, and the associated $t$ and $p$ values are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:summary_mod_4}.

\subsection{Testing significance of independent variables} % (fold)
\label{sub:testing_significance_of_independent_variables}

Under the assumptions specified in Section \ref{sec:assumptions_underlying_regression_results}, the estimates of the coefficients are unbiased, and when the assumptions from Section \ref{sec:testing_model_assumptions} hold, the residual term $\epsilon$ is normally distributed, with constant variance, and free of autocorrelation. With this in place, we can now develop and test hypotheses about any particular coefficient $\beta_j$ in the model. These are conventional statistical hypotheses used to validate and interpret the model; they do not use the audit convention for evaluating material misstatement. Remember that these coefficients are unknown and that we will never know them with certainty. We follow the nine-step approach from Section \ref{sec:the_nine_step_approach}.

\paragraph{Research question} We are particularly interested in determining which independent variables have a significant impact on revenue ($y$). Specifically, we test whether the coefficient of each independent variable is significantly different from zero. If the coefficient of an independent variable is significantly different from zero, we conclude that this variable has a partial effect on revenue, accounting for the effects of all other variables in the model. Conversely, if the coefficient is not significantly different from zero, we conclude that the independent variable does not have a measurable impact on revenue within the context of the model.

\paragraph{Hypotheses} We specify the null hypothesis as

\begin{equation*}
H_0 : \beta_j = 0
%\label{}
\end{equation*}
where $j$ corresponds to any of the $k$ independent variables. That makes the alternative hypothesis

\begin{equation*}
H_1 : \beta_j \neq 0
%\label{}
\end{equation*}


\paragraph{Assumptions} Both sets of assumptions (see Sections \ref{sec:assumptions_underlying_regression_results} and \ref{sec:testing_model_assumptions}) are relevant for the test, as we want to start from an unbiased estimate and the particular traits of the residuals.

\paragraph{Test statistic} We use the \hlblue{$t$ statistic}\index{t statistic}, defined as

\begin{equation}
  t_{\hat{\beta}_j} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{s_{\hat{\beta}_j}}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\beta}_j$ is the estimate of slope coefficient $j$ \nomenclature{$\hat{\beta}_j$}{estimate of slope coefficient $j$}, and $s_{\hat{\beta}_j}$ is its standard deviation.

There is a different $t$ statistic for every coefficient in the regression model.

\paragraph{Distribution} Without further proof, we state that the $t$ statistic follows a $t$ distribution with $n-k-1$ degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Significance level} As before, we will deviate from the standard approach of committing to a significance level and draw our conclusions based on the \emph{$p$ value} for the test.

\paragraph{Value of test statistic} We obtain estimates of the coefficients, their related standard errors, $t$ values, and $p$ values from Table \ref{tab:summary_mod_5}.

\paragraph{Conclusion}

The predictor \ttblue{heatDD} and its interaction with \ttblue{summer} were removed during the backward elimination procedure. 
Among the remaining predictors, only the intercept shows a high $p$ value ($p = 0.525$), yet its inclusion is essential for defining the baseline level of the dependent variable when all predictors equal zero. 
Similarly, while the variable \ttblue{summer} may seem a borderline candidate for exclusion based on significance, its role as an interaction partner is critical: removing it would also eliminate the associated interaction terms with \ttblue{production} and \ttblue{coolDD}, both of which are statistically and substantively significant. 
The variable \ttblue{summer} enables the regression model to differentiate behavior between summer and non-summer periods, affecting both the intercept and the slopes of other predictors in meaningful ways.

\subsection{Testing significance of the model} % (fold)
\label{sub:testing_significance_of_the_model}

In addition to testing the significance of the individual coefficients in the model, we can test whether the model as a whole is significant. This is also a conventional model-validation test rather than an audit test of the recorded amount. It may seem superfluous and, in most cases, it is. Only in rare circumstances are we working with a significant model that incorporates insignificant independent variables. These circumstances do not occur in most auditing applications. In addition to testing the significance of the model as a whole, we can use the information used to compare the models. Once more, we follow the nine-step approach from Section \ref{sec:the_nine_step_approach}.

\paragraph{Research question} We want to know if the changes in the dependent variable are explained by (one of) the independent variable(s). 

\paragraph{Hypotheses} We specify the null hypothesis as

\begin{equation*}
H_0 : \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \ldots = \beta_k = 0
%\label{}
\end{equation*}
where $k$ denotes the number of independent variables. That makes the alternative hypothesis

\begin{equation*}
H_1 : \text{At least one of the } \beta_j \neq 0
%\label{}
\end{equation*}


\paragraph{Assumptions} There are again several assumptions that need to be satisfied for the test statistic, including linearity, randomness, homoskedasticity, normality, and the absence of autocorrelation.

\paragraph{Test statistic} We use the \hlblue{$F$ statistic}\index{F statistic}, which is defined as the ratio between the explained  and unexplained variances. The explained variance is measured by the \hlblue{Mean Square of the Regression}\index{mean square of the regression} or \hlblue{MSR}\index{MSR}, and the unexplained variance by the \hlblue{Mean Square of the Residuals}\index{mean square of the residuals} or \hlblue{MSE}\index{MSE}.

\begin{equation}
  F = \frac{MSR}{MSE}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Distribution} Without further proof, we state that the $F$ statistic follows an $F$ distribution with $k$ and $n-k-1$ degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Significance level} As previously mentioned, we deviate from the standard approach of committing to a significance level ahead of time and then using a critical region. Instead, we draw our conclusion based on the specific $p$ value.

\paragraph{Value of test statistic} The value of the test statistic is $F = 81.8$, with 5 and 30 degrees of freedom, and a $p$ value of less than 2.2e-16.

\paragraph{Conclusion} The value of the test statistic is significant at all reasonable levels. Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis that all the coefficients in the model are equal to zero. The model appears to be significant.

We now want to determine if this deviation is within expected bounds or if it is indicative of contradictory or discontinuing information and therefore requires further investigation.

To measure the uncertainty around a expectation in regression, we use two types of intervals: the \emph{confidence interval}\index{confidence interval}\index{interval!confidence} and the \emph{prediction interval}\index{prediction interval}\index{interval!prediction}. Both intervals give ranges, but they serve different purposes.

\paragraph{Confidence Interval} This interval estimates the range where the average value of the dependent variable is expected to lie, given a certain value of the independent variable. It accounts for the uncertainty in estimating the coefficients of the model but does not account for the random variation around individual observations. 
The confidence interval for a model with one independent variable is given by\footnote{The formula for confidence intervals for multiple linear regression models is more complicated and is best rendered using matrix algebra notation.}
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_* \pm t_{\alpha / 2} \cdot s_e \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(x_* - \bar{x})^2}{(n - 1) s_x^2}}
\end{equation}
\nomenclature{$x_*$}{given value of the independent variable}

The confidence interval thus predicts the average value of $y$ for a given set of values of independent variables, based solely on the model's estimation uncertainty.

For example, when using a model with one single independent variable like \ttblue{mod.0} to create an expectation of $y$ = \ttblue{revenue} from $x$ = \ttblue{production}, in the rare case that we are interested in obtaining an estimate of the average value of $y$ given that $x = 606,400$, we first use Equation \ref{eq:regression_model_0} to estimate $y_*$\footnote{The regression formula from Equation \ref{eq:regression_model_0} is $\hat{y} = 4,897,663 + 18.99x$}
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{y_*} = 4,897,663 + 18.99 \cdot 606,400 = 16,414,429
\end{equation*}
a 99\% confidence interval around this estimate is then
\begin{equation*}
  16,414,429 \pm 2.728 \cdot 4,112,487 \sqrt{\frac{1}{36} + \frac{(606,400 - 471,663)^2}{35 \cdot 29,945,297,385}} =
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
16,414,429 \pm 11,470,711
\end{equation*}
\paragraph{Prediction Interval} This interval gives the range in which an individual observation of $y$ is expected to fall, for a certain value of the independent variable. In addition to model uncertainty (accounted for by the confidence interval), the prediction interval includes random data variation, capturing the natural scatter of data around the regression line. The formula for the prediction interval is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:prediction_interval}
y_* \pm t_{\alpha / 2} \cdot s_e \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(x_* - \bar{x})^2}{(n - 1) s_x^2}}
\end{equation}

Here, the extra $+1$ under the square root accounts for the additional variation of an individual observation around its expectation, rather than the average.

The difference between the confidence interval and the prediction interval is visualized in Figure \ref{fig:H7_conf_and_pred_intervals}. The confidence interval is narrower than the prediction interval, as it only accounts for uncertainty in the model coefficients and predicts the \emph{mean response}. The prediction interval is wider, as it includes both uncertainty in the model coefficients and unexplained data variation, giving a range for a \emph{single new observation}.

\section{Evaluation} % (fold)
\label{sec:auditor_response}

In the preceding sections, we developed and statistically validated regression models to predict revenue for future observations. These model-validation procedures use conventional statistical hypotheses and establish whether the model is suitable for prediction. In the Case: US SteamCo, the model was estimated using data from the prior-year period and then applied to the 12 months of the 2014 hold-out period.

The audit evaluation follows two stages. First, the auditor compares the individual and combined recorded amounts with expectations generated by the prior-year model. Differences that are not consistent with the model's prediction uncertainty are identified and investigated. The auditor evaluates the explanations and corroborating evidence and, where appropriate, adjusts the expectations for conditions that were not captured by the model. This first stage must be completed before the regression results are used to draw an audit conclusion.

Second, once the individual and combined differences have been satisfactorily resolved, the auditor assesses the strength and precision of the model and determines the level of assurance obtained. At this stage, the regression model is used as audit evidence in a hypothesis test of whether the recorded amount is materially misstated. The achieved assurance is then considered together with the assessed risk and evidence from other audit procedures to determine whether further substantive procedures are required.

The monthly expectations serve as benchmarks for evaluating the recorded monthly revenues. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:H7_expectations}, most monthly recorded amounts fall within their individual 99\% prediction intervals. One notable exception is March 2014, where recorded revenue falls below the lower prediction limit. This observation requires further investigation. In the case facts, the lower-than-expected revenue may be related to an unexpected storm affecting the East Coast. Such events illustrate that deviations from model expectations may arise from legitimate business circumstances, model limitations, misstatements, or a combination of these factors.

After investigating and, where appropriate, incorporating corroborated explanations for individual monthly deviations, the next step is to evaluate the recorded revenue for the year as a whole. This requires combining the 12 monthly expectations into a single annual expectation and constructing an \hlblue{acceptable difference range}\index{acceptable difference range}\index{Acceptable Difference Range} (ADR)\index{ADR} around it. The acceptable difference range is the range within which the difference between recorded revenue and expected revenue is acceptable without further investigation. It is constructed from the prediction uncertainty of the annual total and therefore reflects the precision of the model when applied to the hold-out year.

Only after the individual and annual differences have been sa\-tis\-factorily resolved do we proceed to the second stage: evaluating the strength of the regression evidence. The auditor determines the assurance provided by the model and decides whether that assurance, together with other audit evidence, is sufficient to reject material misstatement or whether further substantive procedures are required.

\subsection{Combining detail expectations} % (fold)
\label{sub:combining_detail_expectations}

When predictions are made for multiple periods, such as the 12 months in the hold-out year, the annual expectation is obtained by summing the individual monthly expectations:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:annual_expectation}
\hat{y}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} \hat{y}_i.
\end{equation}

Constructing a prediction interval for this annual total requires more care. The annual prediction interval cannot be obtained by summing the lower and upper limits of the monthly prediction intervals. Nor should the annual prediction standard error be calculated merely as the square root of the sum of the squared monthly prediction standard errors. The reason is that all monthly predictions are based on the same estimated regression coefficients. As a result, the estimation errors in the monthly predictions are correlated, even when the future residual errors are uncorrelated.

This distinction is important. Tests for autocorrelation, such as the Breusch--Godfrey test, address correlation in the residual errors. They do not address the dependence between monthly predictions that arises because each prediction uses the same estimated regression coefficients.

For a regression model with one predictor, the monthly expectation for hold-out observation $i$ is

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:monthly_expectation}
\hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_i.
\end{equation}

The annual expectation can therefore be written as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:annual_expectation_one_predictor}
\hat{y}_{\text{total}} = 12\hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \sum_{i=1}^{12} x_i.
\end{equation}

\medskip
The prediction variance of the annual total consists of two components. The first component reflects the uncertainty in the estimated regression coefficients. This component captures the covariance between the monthly expectations caused by using the same estimated coefficients. The second component reflects the future residual variation of the 12 monthly observations.

Under the assumption that the future residuals are uncorrelated and have common variance $\hat{\sigma}^2$, the prediction variance of the annual total is

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:annual_prediction_variance}
\begin{split}
\operatorname{Var}
\left(
y_{\text{total}} - \hat{y}_{\text{total}}
\right)
={}&
12^2\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\beta}_0)
+
\left(
\sum_{i=1}^{12} x_i
\right)^2
\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1) \\
&+
2 \cdot 12
\left(
\sum_{i=1}^{12} x_i
\right)
\operatorname{Cov}(\hat{\beta}_0,\hat{\beta}_1)
+
12\hat{\sigma}^2.
\end{split}
\end{equation}

The formulas in this section assume a regression model with a single predictor. For models with multiple predictors, the same principles apply, but the corresponding formulas require matrix notation, which is beyond the scope of this book.

\medskip
The \hlblue{combined prediction standard error}\index{combined prediction standard error}\nomenclature{$s_{\text{comb}}$}{combined prediction standard error} is therefore

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:combined_prediction_standard_error}
s_{\text{comb}}
=
\sqrt{
\operatorname{Var}
\left(
y_{\text{total}}-\hat{y}_{\text{total}}
\right)
}.
\end{equation}

% \label{eq:combined_prediction_standard_error}

The 99\% acceptable difference range around the annual expectation is then obtained as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:annual_prediction_interval}
\hat{y}_{\text{total}}
\pm
t_{0.995,df}
s_{\text{comb}},
\end{equation}
where $df$ is the residual degrees of freedom of the regression model.

\medskip
For the \emph{Case: US SteamCo}, the annual expectation is obtained by summing the 12 monthly expectations after adjusting the March 2014 expectation for the corroborated winter storm effect.
The resulting annual expectation is:

\begin{equation*}
\hat{y}_{\text{total}} = 190,680,684.
\end{equation*}

Using the variances and covariance of the estimated regression coefficients together with the residual variance yields a combined prediction standard error of

\begin{equation*}
s_{\text{comb}} = 6,323,301.
\end{equation*}

The corresponding annual prediction-variance components are approximately $1.4887 \cdot 10^{13}$ from coefficient uncertainty and $2.5098 \cdot 10^{13}$ from future residual variation. Thus, about 37.2\% of annual prediction variance comes from parameter estimation and 62.8\% from future-period noise.

With a 99\% prediction interval and the appropriate $t$ critical value, the resulting acceptable prediction range for the annual revenue is

\begin{equation*}
173,291,634
\le
y_{\text{total}}
\le
208,069,735.
\end{equation*}

The recorded annual revenue for 2014 equals

\begin{equation*}
y_{\text{recorded}} = 174,778,400,
\end{equation*}
so that the annual difference, defined as recorded revenue minus expected revenue, is

\begin{equation*}
174,778,400 - 190,680,684 = -15,902,284.
\end{equation*}

The annual difference is consistent with the uncertainty measured by the adjusted 99\% acceptable difference range. The substantive analytical procedure therefore does not identify a remaining significant unexplained difference at the annual level, and the auditor can proceed to determine whether the evidence and achieved assurance are sufficient to reject material misstatement.

\medskip
If there is evidence of residual autocorrelation in the hold-out errors, the residual variance component in Equation~\ref{eq:annual_prediction_variance} should be adjusted to reflect that correlation. In the present case, the model diagnostics do not provide sufficient evidence of residual autocorrelation after refinement, so we proceed under the assumption that the future residual errors are uncorrelated.

\subsection{The nine-step approach} % (fold)
\label{sub:the_nine_step_approach}

With the combined revenue expectations calculated and the identified differences satisfactorily resolved, the auditor proceeds to the second stage and assesses the assurance provided by the regression evidence. The question is whether this evidence is sufficient to reject material misstatement of the recorded revenue for the hold-out year.
We will follow the nine-step approach from Section \ref{sec:the_nine_step_approach} to analyze this question.

\paragraph{Step 1. Develop the research question}
The research question here is: 
\begin{quote}
Does the regression evidence provide sufficient assurance to reject the hypothesis that the recorded total revenue for the hold-out year is materially misstated?
\end{quote}
This question guides the choice of statistical test and the interpretation of the results.

\paragraph{Step 2. State the null and alternative hypotheses}
Following the audit convention used in the sampling chapters, we set up two possible scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $H_0$: The recorded revenue for the year is materially misstated. This could take two forms:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $H_{0A}$: The revenue is materially overstated, recorded revenue is greater than true revenue by an amount that exceeds $PM$.
        \item $H_{0B}$: The revenue is materially understated, recorded revenue is less than true revenue by an amount that exceeds $PM$.
    \end{itemize}
    \item $H_1$: The recorded revenue for the year is not materially misstated.
\end{itemize}
Here we define $PM$, the \hlblue{materiality}\index{materiality} threshold, representing the degree of revenue deviation that would indicate a material misstatement.

\paragraph{Step 3. Consider statistical assumptions}
This test relies on several assumptions about the data:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Independence:} Monthly revenue figures should ideally be independent. If dependencies exist (e.g., seasonality or trends), they should be accounted for in the model.
    \item \emph{Normality:} The errors in the revenue predictions should be ap\-prox\-i\-mate\-ly normally distributed.
    \item \emph{Consistency in model parameters:} The model’s parameters and residual variance are assumed to hold for the combined predictions over the year.
\end{itemize}
These assumptions ensure that the test’s distribution and the interval calculations are valid, providing a reliable assessment of the recorded revenue.

\paragraph{Step 4. Choose an appropriate test and its test statistic}
Given that we are testing whether the observed revenue total significantly deviates from its expected range, we use a \emph{one-sample test for the total revenue}, comparing it to the predicted total. The test statistic $T$\nomenclature{$T$}{test statistic for combined prediction} for this assessment is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:test_statistic}
  T = \frac{y_{\text{recorded}} - \hat{y}_{\text{total}}}{s_{comb}}
\end{equation}

\begin{tabbing}
\hspace*{1.2cm}\=\hspace*{1.4cm}\=\hspace*{0.2cm}\= \kill
where \> $\hat{y}_{\text{total}}$ \> : \> combined expected revenue \\
      \> $y_{\text{recorded}}$     \> : \> recorded revenue \\
      \> $s_{comb}$     \> : \> standard error of the combined prediction (Eq. \ref{eq:annual_prediction_interval})
\end{tabbing}

\paragraph{Step 5. Derive the distribution of the test statistic under the hypotheses}
Under the alternative hypothesis $H_1$, which assumes that the recorded revenue is not materially misstated, the test statistic $T$ follows a central $t$~distribution with $n-k-1$ degrees of freedom. This distribution accounts for the uncertainty in the model's coefficient estimates and the natural variability around the predicted revenue.

Under the null hypothesis $H_0$, where the recorded revenue is materially overstated or understated, the test statistic follows a non-central $t$~distribution. Since $T$ is a standardized statistic, the non-centrality parameter must also be standardized. At the performance-materiality boundary, it is defined as

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:standardized_noncentrality_parameter}
  \lambda_{PM} = \frac{PM}{s_{comb}} ,
\end{equation}

where $PM$ is performance materiality expressed in monetary units and $s_{comb}$ is the standard error of the combined prediction, also expressed in monetary units. The ratio $\lambda_{PM}$ is therefore dimensionless.

Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
\item Under $H_{0A}$ (material overstatement), the non-centrality parameter is $+\lambda_{PM}$.
\item Under $H_{0B}$ (material understatement), the non-centrality parameter is $-\lambda_{PM}$.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
The non-central $t$~distribution under $H_0$ allows us to calculate the probability of incorrectly rejecting material misstatement when a material misstatement of size $PM$ exists. This is the Type I error risk $\alpha$; audit assurance is $1-\alpha$.

\paragraph{Step 6. Select a significance level}
A 99\% prediction interval has a central tail probability of 0.01. In the audit hypothesis test, this probability concerns the alternative hypothesis of no material misstatement and therefore represents Type II error risk $\beta$, or efficiency risk. It is not the audit significance level $\alpha$. The Type I error risk $\alpha$ is determined under the non-central distribution at the materiality boundary, and assurance equals $1-\alpha$.

\paragraph{Step 7. Determine the decision bounds}\footnote{
Traditional presentations of hypothesis testing often refer to the \emph{critical region}. In this chapter we instead refer to the \emph{decision bounds} because the audit null hypothesis assumes a material misstatement. Consequently, the rejection region lies between the bounds rather than in the tails of the distribution. Referring to the bounds rather than the critical region avoids confusion and emphasizes that observations outside the bounds do not indicate that the test has failed; they indicate that there is insufficient evidence to reject material misstatement.}
\nopagebreak[4]
The Acceptable Difference Range is defined by the 99\% prediction interval around the expected difference between the expected and recorded revenue:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:acceptable_difference_range}
\text{ADR} = \pm t_{0.005} \cdot s_{\text{comb}}
\end{equation}

where $t_{0.005}$ denotes the critical value of the $t$~distribution for a two-sided significance level of 1\% with $n-k-1$ degrees of freedom.

\bigskip

The ADR is expressed in monetary units and represents the range of unexplained differences that are considered acceptable. Dividing the ADR by $s_{\text{comb}}$ gives the corresponding decision bounds for the standardized test statistic $T$:

\[
T = \pm t_{0.005}.
\]

A test statistic that falls between these bounds provides sufficient evidence to reject the audit null hypothesis of material misstatement. Test statistics below the lower bound or above the upper bound do not provide sufficient evidence to reject $H_0$.

\paragraph{Step 8. Compute the value of the test statistic from the sample}
Using the observed revenue for the hold-out year, we compute the test statistic $T$ by substituting the recorded revenue $y_i$ and the combined standard error $s_{\text{comb}}$ into the formula.

\paragraph{Step 9. Conclude on the test}
Finally, we evaluate the test statistic against the decision bounds:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{If $T$ lies between the decision bounds}, and the achieved assurance is sufficient, we reject $H_0$ and conclude that sufficient evidence has been obtained that the recorded revenue is not materially misstated.
    \item \emph{If $T$ lies outside the decision bounds}, we do not reject $H_0$. The procedure has not obtained sufficient evidence that the recorded revenue is not materially misstated. Consequently, further investigation or other substantive procedures are required.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Level of assurance obtained} % (fold)
\label{sub:level_of_assurance_obtained}

After all relevant individual and annual differences have been identified, investigated, and satisfactorily resolved, the auditor assesses the strength of the regression model as audit evidence. The decision bounds translate the model's prediction uncertainty, performance materiality, and the auditor's risk parameters into a statistical decision rule. A precise model provides more assurance than an imprecise model for the same performance materiality and observed difference.

Consistent with Section~\ref{sub:acceptable_difference} in Volume 2, we define the difference as

\begin{equation}
D = y_{\text{recorded}} - \hat{y}_{\text{total}}.
\end{equation}

A positive difference indicates that the recorded amount exceeds the expectation and is therefore relevant when considering a possible overstatement. A negative difference indicates that the recorded amount is below the expectation and is therefore relevant when considering a possible understatement.

The standardized difference is

\begin{equation}
T = \frac{D}{s_{\text{comb}}},
\end{equation}
where $s_{\text{comb}}$ is the combined prediction standard error. If the account is not materially misstated, $T$ follows a central $t$ distribution with $n - k - 1$ degrees of freedom.

Let

\begin{equation}
\delta = \frac{PM}{s_{\text{comb}}}
\end{equation}
be the standardized non-centrality parameter\nomenclature{$\delta$}{standardized non-centrality parameter of the non-central $t$ distribution}, where $PM$ is performance materiality expressed in monetary units. Because both $PM$ and $s_{\text{comb}}$ are expressed in monetary units, $\delta$ is dimensionless, like the test statistic $T$. If revenue is materially overstated by $PM$, the distribution of $T$ shifts to the right and follows a non-central $t$ distribution with non-centrality parameter $+\delta$. If revenue is materially understated by $PM$, the distribution shifts to the left and follows a non-central $t$ distribution with non-centrality parameter $-\delta$.

As discussed in Section~\ref{sub:acceptable_difference} in Volume 2, the decision bounds may be determined by controlling either efficiency risk or effectiveness risk. If the auditor controls efficiency risk, the monetary bounds are based on the central $t$ distribution under the assumption of no material misstatement. For an efficiency risk of $\beta$, these bounds are

\begin{equation}
\left[
t_{\beta/2,df}s_{\text{comb}},
t_{1-\beta/2,df}s_{\text{comb}}
\right].
\end{equation}

This limits the probability of investigating a difference when the account is not materially misstated. In that case, the level of assurance is not fixed in advance. It follows from the width of the bounds, the precision of the model, and performance materiality.

Let $c_L$ and $c_U$ denote the lower and upper standardized decision bounds. The achieved assurance for detecting a material overstatement is the upper-tail probability

\begin{equation}
P(T > c_U \mid T \sim t_{df,\delta}).
\end{equation}

The achieved assurance for detecting a material understatement is the lower-tail probability

\begin{equation}
P(T < c_L \mid T \sim t_{df,-\delta}).
\end{equation}

Using the chapter's case settings $PM = 15,\!000,\!000$ and $\beta = 0.01$, the achieved assurance is 36.9\% for detecting a material overstatement and 36.9\% for detecting a material understatement.

These probabilities express the likelihood that a material misstatement of size $PM$ would produce a difference outside the decision bounds. Their complement, $\alpha$, is the risk that a material misstatement of size $PM$ nevertheless produces a difference between the decision bounds, causing the auditor to incorrectly reject the audit null hypothesis of material misstatement. Under the audit convention, assurance is therefore $1-\alpha$.

\bigskip
Alternatively, the auditor may determine the decision bounds by controlling $\alpha$ directly. In that case, the bounds are chosen to provide the desired level of assurance, $1-\alpha$, against incorrectly rejecting material misstatement. This fixes the desired level of assurance, but the resulting range may become narrow when the model is imprecise. This increases $\beta$, the Type II error risk of failing to reject $H_0$ when the recorded amount is not materially misstated, and therefore increases the probability of investigating differences caused merely by model imprecision.

The achieved level of assurance should therefore be interpreted together with the precision of the model, performance materiality, the assessed risk of material misstatement, and evidence obtained from other audit procedures. Resolving all unexpected differences is necessary, but does not, by itself, establish that sufficient audit evidence has been obtained. If the achieved assurance is sufficient, the auditor may reject the hypothesis of material misstatement. If it falls short of the required level, the regression procedure may still contribute audit evidence, but further substantive procedures are required.
