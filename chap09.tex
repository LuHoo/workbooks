\chapter{Goodness of fit} % (fold)
\label{cha:goodness_of_fit}

\begin{quote}
`If the facts don't fit the theory, change the facts.\\
Albert Einstein (1879-1955)
\end{quote}

In the previous chapter, we used regression analysis to study relationships between variables and to evaluate how well statistical models described observed data. An important question that arose throughout that discussion was whether a proposed model provided an adequate representation of reality. Measures such as the coefficient of determination and analyses of residuals offered ways to assess the quality of a model's fit. More generally, statisticians often need methods for determining whether observed data are consistent with an expected pattern or distribution. This is the central idea behind goodness-of-fit analysis.

Goodness-of-fit techniques compare observed outcomes with those predicted by a theoretical model. When the observed and expected values are sufficiently close, the model may be considered plausible. Large discrepancies, however, may indicate that the model is inappropriate, that important factors have been overlooked, or that the data contain unusual observations. As a result, goodness-of-fit methods are widely used in fields ranging from scientific research and quality control to economics and public policy.

One particularly interesting application involves the detection of fraud. Auditors and forensic accountants often examine whether numerical data conform to patterns that commonly occur in naturally generated datasets. A well-known example is Benford’s Law, which predicts the frequency with which different digits appear as the leading digit in many real-world collections of numbers. Although deviations from Benford’s Law do not prove wrongdoing, they can serve as useful warning signals that warrant further investigation. In this chapter, we introduce the concepts and methods of goodness-of-fit testing, with special attention to Benford’s Law and its role in auditing and fraud detection.

\section*{Case Study: Benford's Law and the Search for Irregularities}
\sloppy

Imagine opening a book of logarithm tables in the nineteenth century. The first pages are noticeably more worn than the later pages. This curious observation led astronomer and mathematician Simon Newcomb to an unexpected discovery. In 1881, Newcomb noted that numbers beginning with the digit 1 appeared more frequently than numbers beginning with larger digits. He published a short article proposing that naturally occurring numbers do not have uniformly distributed leading digits.

Newcomb's observation attracted little attention until physicist Frank Benford independently rediscovered the phenomenon almost fifty years later. Benford collected more than 20,000 observations from diverse sources, including river lengths, population figures, and physical constants. He found a remarkably consistent pattern: approximately 30\% of numbers begin with the digit 1, while fewer than 5\% begin with the digit 9. Today, this pattern is known as \emph{Benford's Law}.

One reason auditors find Benford's Law interesting is that fabricated numbers often fail to follow this natural pattern. Human beings tend to distribute digits more evenly than they occur in real datasets. As a result, deviations from Benford's distribution can serve as indicators of unusual activity requiring further investigation.

A famous example involves HealthSouth Corporation, one of the largest accounting frauds in U.S. history. During the late 1990s and early 2000s, executives allegedly created numerous fraudulent journal entries to inflate reported earnings. Subsequent analyses showed that the fabricated entries exhibited digit patterns that differed from those predicted by Benford's Law. Researchers have argued that systematic application of digit analysis could have identified unusual patterns much earlier in the audit process.

Benford's Law has also been applied to public-sector data. During the European sovereign debt crisis, researchers examined economic statistics reported by several countries, including Greece, to investigate whether reported figures displayed unusual digit distributions. While such analyses cannot prove manipulation, they can highlight datasets that deserve closer scrutiny and additional audit procedures.

These examples illustrate both the power and the limitations of Benford's Law. A departure from the expected distribution is not evidence of fraud by itself. Legitimate datasets may deviate from Benford's pattern for many reasons, including small sample sizes or inappropriate data characteristics. Nevertheless, Benford analysis provides auditors with a simple and inexpensive screening tool that can help focus attention on areas of elevated risk.

The aim is to investigate whether customer satisfaction has changed in the subsequent year.

For a goodness-of-fit test, the hypotheses examine whether the distribution of satisfaction in the follow-up year is the same as the distribution observed in the base year. Because all 625 customers were surveyed in the base year, the observed proportions are equal to the population proportions for that year. These proportions therefore serve as the hypothesized probabilities under the null hypothesis.

Let $\pi_i$ denote the proportion of customers in the follow-up year belonging to satisfaction category $i$. The null hypothesis is
\begin{equation*}
    H_0 : \pi_1 = 0.40, \pi_2 = 0.16, \pi_3 = 0.44
\end{equation*}

The alternative hypothesis states that the distribution of customer satisfaction has changed:

\begin{equation*}
    H_1 : \text{At least one of the } \pi_i \text{ differs from its hypothesized value}
\end{equation*}

The follow-up study found that, from a sample of 40 customers, 15 were (very) dissatisfied, 12 were neutral, and 13 were (very) satisfied. The observed frequencies are summarized in Table \ref{tab:client_satisfaction_followup}.

Under the null hypothesis, the population proportions are

\begin{equation*}
    \pi_1 = 0.40,\qquad \pi_2 = 0.16,\qquad \pi_3 = 0.44
\end{equation*}

The expected frequencies are therefore obtained by multiplying these proportions by the sample size $n=40$:

\begin{equation*}
E_1 = 40(0.40) = 16.0,\qquad
E_2 = 40(0.16) = 6.4,\qquad
E_3 = 40(0.44) = 17.6.
\end{equation*}

The observed and expected frequencies are shown in Table \ref{tab:gof_expected}. Although frequencies are often reported as whole numbers, the expected frequencies used in a chi-square test may be fractional. These expected frequencies should not be rounded before calculating the test statistic, as rounding can affect the result of the test.

The chi-square test statistic is

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\chi^2
&=
\sum_{i=1}^{k}
\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i} \\[1ex]
&=
\frac{(15-16)^2}{16}
+
\frac{(12-6.4)^2}{6.4}
+
\frac{(13-17.6)^2}{17.6} \\[1ex]
&=
0.063 + 4.900 + 1.202 \\[1ex]
&=
6.165.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:chi_square_test_statistic}
\end{equation}

Since there are $k=3$ categories and no parameters are estimated from the sample, the number of degrees of freedom is

\begin{equation*}
df = k-1 = 3-1 = 2.
\end{equation*}

For a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$, the critical value is $\chi^2_{0.05,2} = 5.991$. Because

\begin{equation*}
\chi^2 = 6.165 > \chi^2_{0.05,2} = 5.991,
\end{equation*}
the null hypothesis is rejected.

\bigskip
Equivalently, the $p$ value is

\begin{equation*}
    p = P(\chi^2_2 \geq 6.165) \approx 0.046.
\end{equation*}

Since $p < 0.05$, the null hypothesis is rejected. 

\bigskip
Conclusion: there is sufficient evidence at the 5\% significance level to conclude that the distribution of customer satisfaction in the follow-up year differs from the distribution observed in the base year.

\subsection{The Chi-Square Distribution}

The chi-square goodness-of-fit test is used to determine whether the distribution of a categorical variable corresponds to a hypothesized distribution.

Suppose that a sample of size $n$ is classified into $k$ mutually exclusive categories. Under the null hypothesis, the probability that an observation falls into category $i$ is ($\pi_i$), where

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^{k}\pi_i = 1.
\end{equation*}

Let $O_i$ denote the observed frequency in category $i$. The vector of observed frequencies

\begin{equation*}
    (O_1,O_2,\ldots,O_k)
\end{equation*}

follows a multinomial distribution:

\begin{equation*}
    (O_1,O_2, \ldots, O_k) \sim \text{Multinomial}(n; \pi_1, \pi_2, \ldots, \pi_k).
\end{equation*}

For each category $i$, the expected frequency is

\begin{equation*}
    E_i = n \pi_i .
\end{equation*}

When the sample size is sufficiently large, the multinomial distribution can be approximated by a multivariate normal distribution. Consequently, the standardized deviations $\frac{O_i-E_i}
{\sqrt{E_i}}$ are approximately standard normally distributed:

\begin{equation*}
    Z_i \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} N(0,1).
\end{equation*}

The chi-square test statistic is obtained by summing the squared standardized deviations across all categories:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i = 1}^{k} \left(\frac{O_i - E_i}{\sqrt{E_i}}\right)^2
\end{equation*}

Under the null hypothesis and for sufficiently large sample sizes, the test statistic follows approximately a chi-square distribution. The number of degrees of freedom equals the number of categories minus one, minus the number of parameters estimated from the sample:
\begin{equation*}
    df = k - p - 1
\end{equation*}
where $k$ is the number of categories and $p$ is the number of parameters estimated from the data.

\bigskip
To apply the chi-square goodness-of-fit test, the following conditions should be satisfied:

\begin{itemize}
\item The sample contains at least 20 observations.
\item The observations are independent.
\item The probability that an observation falls into a particular category remains constant throughout the sampling process.
\item The expected frequency in each category is sufficiently large.
\end{itemize}

The final condition deserves special attention. The chi-square distribution is derived as the distribution of a sum of squared standard normal random variables. Therefore, the normal approximation used in the derivation must be adequate for each category. A commonly used rule of thumb is:

\begin{itemize}
\item The expected frequency in every category should be at least 5.
\end{itemize}

This rule is often considered conservative. Cochran (1963) proposed a more flexible guideline. According to this guideline, the chi-square approximation may still be used provided that

\begin{itemize}
\item $E_i \geq 1$ for every category, and
\item at least 80\% of the categories satisfy $E_i \geq 5$.
\end{itemize}

The shape of the chi-square distribution depends on the number of degrees of freedom. As the number of categories increases, the degrees of freedom increase and the distribution becomes more symmetric. For small degrees of freedom, the chi-square distribution is strongly right-skewed; as the degrees of freedom increase, it gradually approaches a normal distribution.

\subsection{Chi-Square Test and Benford's Law}

Before applying a chi-square goodness-of-fit test to assess compliance with
Benford's Law, it is important to verify that the data are suitable for such
an analysis. Benford's Law does not apply to every numerical data set. It is
most commonly observed in naturally occurring numerical data that span several
orders of magnitude and are not constrained by artificial limits.

In practice, the following conditions should be considered:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The data should not be restricted by fixed minimum or maximum values.
    For example, if the weekly turnover of a snack bar always lies between
    6,000 and 9,500, only numbers beginning with the digits 6, 7, 8, or 9
    can occur. Such a restriction prevents the first-digit distribution from
    following Benford's Law.

    \item The values should represent measured or naturally occurring quantities
    rather than assigned identifiers. Numbers such as product codes, item
    numbers, telephone numbers, or social security numbers are generated by
    design and are therefore not expected to follow Benford's Law.

    \item The values should not be heavily influenced by human preferences or
    conventions. For example, retail prices frequently end in digits such as
    95 or 99. These so-called \textit{psychological prices} can create digit
    patterns that differ substantially from those predicted by Benford's Law.
\end{itemize}

Additional practical guidelines are often recommended when performing
Benford analyses:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The data set should contain a sufficiently large number of
    observations, preferably at least 1,000 values.
    \item Values smaller than 10 are typically excluded when analysing
    first-two-digit frequencies.
    \item Numbers should contain at least four digits when first-two-digit
    tests are performed.
\end{itemize}

These guidelines are particularly relevant for tests based on the first two
digits. Values below 10 do not possess two significant leading digits, and a
large sample size is required to ensure that expected frequencies are
sufficiently large for the chi-square approximation to be valid. A commonly
used rule is that each expected frequency should be at least 5.

If a population contains both positive and negative values, the positive and
negative observations are usually analysed separately because they may arise
from different generating processes.

\subsection*{Hypotheses}

Let $\pi_i$ denote the probability that the first digit is equal to $i$. Under Benford's Law,
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
    (\pi_1,\pi_2,\ldots,\pi_9) = (&0.3010,\,0.1761,\,0.1249,\,0.0969,\,0.0792,\,0.0669,\,0.0580,\, \\
    &0.0512,\,0.0458).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
The hypotheses for the chi-square goodness-of-fit test are

\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
    H_0 : (\pi_1,\pi_2,\ldots,\pi_9) = (&0.3010,\,0.1761,\,0.1249,\,0.0969,\,0.0792,\,0.0669, \\
    &\,0.0580,\,0.0512,\,0.0458).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
    H_0 : (\pi_1,\pi_2,\ldots,\pi_9) \neq (&0.3010,\,0.1761,\,0.1249,\,0.0969,\,0.0792,\,0.0669, \\
    &\,0.0580,\,0.0512,\,0.0458).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

In words,

\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
H_0 : &\hspace{0.5em} \text{The first-digit distribution of the inventory data follows } \\
&\hspace{0.5em} \text{Benford's Law,} \\
H_1 : &\hspace{0.5em} \text{The first-digit distribution of the inventory data does not follow} \\
&\hspace{0.5em} \text{Benford's Law. }
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

\subsection*{Calculation of the Test Statistic}

The chi-square goodness-of-fit test statistic is calculated according to Equation \ref{eq:chi_square_test_statistic}:

\begin{equation*}
    \chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{9}
\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i},
\end{equation*}
where \(O_i\) denotes the observed frequency of first digit \(i\) and
\(E_i\) denotes the corresponding expected frequency under Benford's Law.

The statistic measures the overall discrepancy between the observed and
expected distributions. Large values of \(\chi^2\) indicate that the observed
frequencies differ substantially from those predicted by Benford's Law.

\subsection*{Critical Value and Decision Rule}

Under the null hypothesis, the test statistic follows approximately a
chi-square distribution with $df = k-1 = 9-1 = 8$ degrees of freedom, where $k$ is the number of possible first digits.

For a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$, the critical value is
$\chi^2_{0.05,8}=15.51.$

Figure~\ref{fig:H9_chisq_dist_df8} illustrates the chi-square
distribution with 8 degrees of freedom. Values of the test statistic that
fall to the right of the critical value belong to the rejection region.

    \caption{Chi-square distribution with df=8}
    \label{fig:H9_chisq_dist_df8}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

Because $\chi^2 = 19.80 > 15.51$, the test statistic falls within the rejection region. Therefore, the null hypothesis is rejected at the 5\% significance level.

The data provide sufficient statistical evidence to conclude that the
distribution of first digits in the inventory data differs from the
distribution predicted by Benford's Law.

It is important to note that rejecting the null hypothesis does not prove
the existence of fraud, error, or manipulation. The result merely indicates
that the observed digit frequencies are unlikely to have arisen from a
population that follows Benford's Law. The source of the discrepancy must
therefore be investigated further.

\subsection*{Identifying Sources of Deviation}

An advantage of the chi-square statistic is that its individual components
can be examined separately. Each quantity

\[
\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}
\]

represents the contribution of digit \(i\) to the overall test statistic.

Table~\ref{tab:chi_square_calculation} shows that most of the chi-square
value is generated by the digits 6 and 3. Their contributions are 12.62 and 5.59, respectively. Together they account for more than \(90\%\) of the total
test statistic.

A closer inspection reveals that numbers beginning with the digit 6 occur
substantially more often than expected, whereas numbers beginning with the
digit 3 occur substantially less often than expected.

From an auditing perspective, excessive frequencies are often of greater
interest than deficient frequencies. Consequently, the unusually high number
of observations beginning with the digit 6 would normally receive priority
for further investigation.

A natural next step is to analyse the first two digits rather than only the
first digit. This may reveal whether the excess frequency is concentrated in
particular ranges such as 60--69 or even in specific two-digit combinations.

For first-two-digit analysis, Benford's Law predicts that the probability of
observing the two-digit combination \(ij\) is

\[
P(ij)
=
\log_{10}
\left(
1+\frac{1}{ij}
\right),
\tag{11.4}
\]

where

\[
ij = 10,11,12,\ldots,99.
\]

This finer level of analysis can help identify the specific values that are
responsible for the departure from Benford's Law.
